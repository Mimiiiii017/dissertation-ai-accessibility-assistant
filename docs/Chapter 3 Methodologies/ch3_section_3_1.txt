Chapter 3: Methodology and Implementation

3.1 Chapter Introduction
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This chapter presents the methodology employed in the development and evaluation
of an AI-powered web accessibility assistant delivered as a Visual Studio Code
(VS Code) extension.  It provides a comprehensive account of the system design,
the construction of the retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline, the prompt
engineering strategy, the benchmarking infrastructure, and the statistical
evaluation framework through which the research hypotheses are tested.  The
chapter is structured to guide the reader from the high-level research rationale
through to the detailed implementation decisions and the procedures followed
during each phase of testing.

The study adopts a quantitative, deductive approach.  Deductive reasoning
proceeds from established general principles — in this case, the published
accessibility success criteria of WCAG 2.2 — and applies them to a specific
domain of investigation, namely the automated detection of violations in real-
world web source code.  A fixed set of ground-truth annotated test fixtures
serves as the experimental apparatus against which model outputs are measured,
enabling objective calculation of precision, recall, F1 score, and the Matthews
Correlation Coefficient (MCC).  This methodology is consistent with established
practice in the evaluation of code intelligence tools [11] and aligns with the
reproducibility guidelines of Pineau et al. [12], which emphasise controlled
variables, fixed seeds, and clearly documented parameter choices as prerequisites
for verifiable results.

The research is implemented through three sequential evaluation phases.  Phase 1
benchmarks eighteen cloud-hosted large language models under identical inference
conditions to identify the optimal model.  Phase 2 conducts a 2×2 factorial
ablation study to isolate the independent and combined contributions of RAG and
chain-of-thought reasoning.  Phase 3 applies the winning configuration to the
full 41-fixture test suite for detailed performance characterisation.  An
ancillary NASA Task Load Index (NASA-TLX) computational study validates the
cognitive-load prediction framework that will underpin the planned human
participant study described in Chapter 4.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.1.1  Research Objective, Research Questions, and Hypotheses
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The primary objective of this research is to investigate whether an LLM, when
grounded in a domain-specific WCAG 2.2 knowledge base via a RAG architecture,
can reliably detect accessibility violations across real-world web source files
at a level of semantic accuracy that exceeds deterministic rule-based tools, and
whether higher detection accuracy is associated with a measurable reduction in
the cognitive workload experienced by developers during accessibility audits.

The scale of web accessibility barriers provides strong practical motivation for
this objective.  The World Health Organization (2023) estimates that approximately
1.3 billion people — 16 per cent of the global population — live with some form
of significant disability [19].  Despite international legal requirements,
including the European Accessibility Act 2025 and the UK Public Sector Bodies
Accessibility Regulations 2018, widespread inaccessibility persists on the web.
The WebAIM Million report (2024), which analyses the home pages of the top one
million websites annually, found that 95.9 per cent contained automatically
detectable WCAG failures, with a mean of 56.8 accessibility errors per page [20].
This figure is consistent with the large-scale audit conducted by Martins and
Duarte (2024), who observed an average of 30 errors per page across 2.88 million
pages from 166,311 websites [4], and with Fernandes et al. (2023), who found a
mean of 51 errors on healthcare web resource home pages [5].

For developers, the typical accessibility remediation workflow involves repeated
context-switching between the code editor, browser-based checkers, and WCAG
documentation — a pattern that fragments attention and increases cognitive load.
This research adopts the shift-left testing principle, which advocates embedding
quality and compliance checks at the point of authorship rather than deferring
them to a separate audit phase (Humble and Farley, 2010) [21].  Hellendoorn et
al. (2021) demonstrated empirically that long time gaps between related
development activities are associated with decreased productivity and increased
error rates [18], providing additional motivation for the IDE-integrated approach
adopted here.

Brown et al. (2020) demonstrated that large foundation models exhibit substantial
zero-shot and few-shot reasoning capability without task-specific fine-tuning
[22], suggesting that a capable model can reason over domain-specific rules when
supplied with appropriate context.  The RAG architecture introduced by Lewis et
al. (2020) grounds model outputs in an authoritative external knowledge base,
addressing the hallucination risk identified during pilot testing and ensuring
that WCAG 2.2 guidance is current regardless of the model's training data
cut-off [23].

Three research questions guide the investigation:

    RQ1: To what extent can a retrieval-augmented large language model detect
         WCAG 2.2 Level A and AA accessibility violations in real-world HTML,
         CSS, JavaScript, and TypeScript source files, as measured by F1
         score and Matthews Correlation Coefficient against a manually
         validated ground-truth concept pool?

    RQ2: What is the independent and combined contribution of (a) retrieval-
         augmented generation and (b) chain-of-thought reasoning to
         accessibility violation detection accuracy across a diverse cohort
         of eighteen cloud-hosted language models?

    RQ3: Is there a statistically significant negative correlation between
         an AI accessibility tool's detection accuracy (F1 score) and the
         subjective cognitive workload (NASA-TLX raw workload score)
         experienced by developers when performing accessibility audits with
         versus without the tool?

Two principal hypotheses are proposed in direct correspondence with RQ1 and RQ3:

    H1: An LLM-backed VS Code extension augmented with a domain-specific
        WCAG 2.2 knowledge base produces significantly higher violation-
        detection accuracy (measured by F1 score) than a baseline LLM
        operating without knowledge-base grounding, across HTML, CSS,
        JavaScript, and TypeScript source files containing empirically
        motivated accessibility errors.

    H2: Higher detection accuracy (F1 score) is negatively correlated with
        developer cognitive load (NASA-TLX raw workload score), such that a
        more capable extension lowers the mental and temporal demand of
        accessibility audits.

RQ2 is addressed through the 2×2 ablation study (Section 3.5.2) and does not
generate a directional hypothesis because the relative contributions of RAG and
chain-of-thought reasoning were not known a priori; it is treated as an
exploratory research question resolved empirically.

Each component of the system implementation directly operationalises one or more
of the research questions and hypotheses above.  The VS Code extension constitutes the experimental
treatment.  The RAG pipeline provides the retrieval augmentation whose
contribution is isolated through ablation.  The benchmarking infrastructure
produces the ground-truth-validated performance metrics against which cognitive-
load estimates are subsequently correlated.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.1.2  High-Level System Architecture
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The system is composed of three tightly coupled layers that together form the
research bed through which both hypotheses are evaluated.

The first layer is the VS Code Extension (TypeScript), which serves as the
user-facing research instrument.  It provides three commands accessible from
the editor command palette: Analyse File, which triggers the full accessibility
analysis pipeline; NASA-TLX Estimate, which generates a predicted cognitive-load
score for the active file; and Select Analysis Profile, which allows the operator
to switch between inference presets.  The extension intercepts developer actions
in the editor, extracts the active file's content, and coordinates the subsequent
retrieval and inference calls.

The second layer is the RAG Service (Python/FastAPI), which maintains a
persistent ChromaDB vector store populated with domain-specific WCAG 2.2
documentation and ARIA authoring guidance.  It exposes a /retrieve HTTP
endpoint on port 8000 and responds to queries with ranked document chunks ranked
by semantic and lexical relevance.

The third layer is the LLM Inference Relay (Ollama, port 11434), which routes
generation requests to the selected language model.  During production use, a
fixed cloud-hosted model (gpt-oss:120b-cloud) is used.  During evaluation, all
eighteen benchmark models were routed through the same relay to ensure identical
network path and retry logic.

3.1.3  End-to-End Pipeline Workflow
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The following describes the complete sequence of operations from the point at
which a developer invokes the Analyse File command to the point at which
formatted results are displayed in the extension panel and logged as VS Code
diagnostic markers.

Step 1 — File Extraction.  The active document in the VS Code editor is
identified and its full text content is extracted.  The language identifier
(html, css, javascript, typescript, typescriptreact) is determined from the
document's registered language mode.

Step 2 — Baseline Static Checks.  A set of regular-expression-based checks is
run synchronously before any model call (runBaselineChecks, baseline.ts).  These
checks capture unambiguous, pattern-detectable violations such as missing
document titles and empty alt attributes.  Results from this stage are merged
with AI-detected issues in the final output.

Step 3 — Code Excerpt Construction.  The full document is passed to
buildRelevantExcerpt() (excerptBuilder.ts), which trims extraneous whitespace
and constructs a token-efficient representation of the file content for
inclusion in the prompt.

Step 4 — RAG Query Construction.  The file's language identifier and excerpt
are passed to buildRagQuery() (ragQueryBuilder.ts), which assembles one or more
targeted retrieval queries.  For HTML files, five distinct sweep queries are
dispatched in parallel, each targeting a different WCAG category (link names,
button names, form labels, images and headings, ARIA references).  This approach
was adopted after early pilot experiments confirmed that a single generic query
fails to surface relevant guidance for the full breadth of violations present in
a dense HTML fixture.  For CSS, JavaScript, and TypeScript files, a single
focused query is constructed from language-specific ARIA and accessibility
keywords.

Step 5 — RAG Retrieval.  Queries are dispatched to the RAG service at
http://127.0.0.1:8000/retrieve.  For HTML, results from all five queries are
deduplicated and capped at eight chunks.  For other languages, up to three
chunks are returned.  Chunks are combined into a WCAG REFERENCE CONTEXT block
via formatRagContext().  A memory cache keyed on file language, query string,
and excerpt length prevents redundant retrieval calls within the same editing
session.

Step 6 — Prompt Assembly.  The system prompt (SYSTEM_PROMPT), the language-
specific sweep rules, the WCAG reference context, and the code excerpt are
assembled into the final user message by buildAiPrompt() (prompt.ts).

Step 7 — LLM Inference.  The assembled messages are dispatched to Ollama via
ollamaGenerateStream(), which opens a streaming connection to the /api/chat
endpoint.  The fixed production model is gpt-oss:120b-cloud.  The response is
streamed token-by-token to the extension panel for real-time display.

Step 8 — Response Parsing.  As the response arrives, pre-parse stripping removes
any <think>...</think> reasoning blocks emitted by chain-of-thought models.  The
remaining text is passed to parseTextResponse(), which extracts structured issue
blocks containing the violation description, WCAG success criterion, line number,
severity, and suggested remediation.  Duplicate issues referring to the same
element are removed by deduplicateIssues().

Step 9 — Diagnostic Integration.  Each parsed issue is converted to a VS Code
Diagnostic object via aiIssueToDiagnostic() and published to the editor's
diagnostic collection, causing red/yellow underlines to appear at the relevant
source locations.  The formatted panel output is simultaneously written to the
extension webview.
