3.5  Evaluation Framework and Testing Matrix
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.5.1  Preliminary Deployment Feasibility Assessment
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Before the full 18-model cloud benchmark was designed (Section 3.5.2), a
preliminary feasibility assessment was conducted to determine whether local,
on-device model deployment via Ollama could serve as a viable, cost-free
alternative to cloud-API access for evaluation at this scale.

Eight models were tested across all four ablation conditions (RAG+Think,
RAG+No-Think, No-RAG+Think, No-RAG+No-Think) on the html-high fixture — the
highest-density HTML fixture, comprising 51 manually annotated violations.  Four
models were deployed locally through an Ollama server: devstral-small-2:24b,
gpt-oss:20b, phi4-reasoning:14b, and qwen3-coder:30b.  A cloud-hosted counterpart
of each was tested concurrently: devstral-small-2:24b-cloud, gpt-oss:120b-cloud,
kimi-k2.6:cloud, and qwen3-coder:480b-cloud.

Results were unambiguous.  All four local models produced zero valid responses
across all four conditions: every attempt returned the error "Ollama connection
failed: fetch failed", indicating that the local inference server could not be
reached under benchmark load.  For gpt-oss:20b, two of the four conditions also
generated a secondary timeout error ("Ollama timed out after 1200s"), confirming
that even on conditions where a connection was not refused outright, the local
model could not complete inference within a practical time budget.  The four cloud
models, by contrast, completed all attempted conditions and produced valid
structured responses suitable for scoring.  The strongest cloud result in this
initial test was gpt-oss:120b-cloud under the No-RAG+No-Think condition, achieving
F1 = 0.5556 (Precision = 0.952, Recall = 0.392, TP = 20, FP = 1, FN = 31 against
51 ground-truth violations).  kimi-k2.6:cloud timed out on three of its four
conditions owing to extended chain-of-thought latency under the 900-second
benchmark budget; it produced one valid result only under No-RAG+No-Think.

These findings established two practical constraints that shaped all subsequent
evaluation phases.  First, local Ollama deployment is not a reliable inference
infrastructure for multi-model, multi-condition benchmarking: connection failures
during a benchmark run produce zero valid data for the affected conditions, leaving
the study with no means to compare models against one another on those axes.
Second, even from a single high-density fixture, the cloud sub-group demonstrated
measurable F1 variation across conditions and model sizes, confirming that the
2×2 ablation design could discriminate meaningfully between them.  The decision
was therefore taken to restrict Phase 1 to cloud-hosted models only, and to
expand the cloud candidate pool to 18 models drawn from a broader range of
providers (Section 3.5.2).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.5.2  The Testing Matrix: Defining Conditions (RAG-Enhanced vs. Baseline)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The evaluation employs a 2 × 2 fully-crossed ablation design as its testing
matrix.  The two independent variables are the RAG condition — whether the
WCAG knowledge base is consulted during inference — and the reasoning condition
— whether chain-of-thought thinking is enabled in models that support extended
reasoning.  This design yields four conditions:

    Condition A:  RAG ON  + Think ON   (RAG + Think)
    Condition B:  RAG ON  + Think OFF  (RAG + No-Think)
    Condition C:  RAG OFF + Think ON   (No-RAG + Think)
    Condition D:  RAG OFF + Think OFF  (No-RAG + No-Think)

Condition D represents the closest approximation to a vanilla LLM baseline — the
model receives only the raw code excerpt and the system prompt, with no knowledge
base retrieval and no extended reasoning.  Condition A represents the full
experimental treatment.  Comparing Condition A against Condition D isolates the
combined contribution of both interventions; comparing Condition A against
Condition B isolates the marginal contribution of chain-of-thought reasoning;
and comparing Condition A against Condition C isolates the marginal contribution
of retrieval augmentation.

All 18 models were evaluated across all four conditions, yielding 72 model-
condition runs.  Every run was conducted on the same 16 core HTML fixtures for
the ablation comparison; the ground-truth scoring procedure described in Section
3.5.4 was applied identically across all runs.

The 18 models evaluated were: gpt-oss:120b-cloud; glm-5; kimi-k2.5; qwen3.5:397b;
deepseek-v3.2; minimax-m2; nemotron-3-nano; gemini-2.5-flash; gemini-2.5-pro;
llama-4-scout; llama-4-maverick; qwen3:235b; step2-16k; qwq-plus; nova-lite;
nova-pro; moonshot-v2; and claude-3.7-sonnet.

Notable patterns in the results include the following.  gpt-oss:120b achieves its
highest composite score under RAG + Think (72.0%), confirming that both
interventions make positive independent contributions for this model.  kimi-k2.5
demonstrates a particularly pronounced dependency on thinking: it is the highest
MCC-scoring model under RAG + Think (MCC = 0.370, balanced accuracy = 83.5%) but
degrades sharply when reasoning is suppressed.  Qwen3.5 demonstrates a
counterintuitive anomaly: under No-RAG + Think it collapses to three true
positives — near-total detection failure — despite being a generally strong
model; this is attributable to reasoning amplifying the hallucination tendency
in the absence of grounding context.  minimax-m2 achieves zero false positives
in all four conditions — the only model with perfect specificity at the cost of
reduced recall.  nemotron-3-nano generates 80 false positives under RAG + Think,
indicating that the knowledge base context triggers over-eagerness in this
model's smaller-scale reasoning.

Based on these results, gpt-oss:120b operating under RAG + Think was selected as
the production configuration for the deployed extension.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.5.3  Technical Performance Metrics
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Scoring each model's output against the ground-truth concept pool produces four
counts per fixture: true positives (TP), false positives (FP), false negatives
(FN), and true negatives (TN).  Rather than direct element-to-element matching,
a concept-based scoring approach is used: each detected issue is mapped to the
closest ground-truth concept via normalised string matching against the canonical
concept descriptors in the ground-truth.ts pool.  An issue is counted as a TP
if the cosine-normalised similarity between its serialised description and a
concept pool entry exceeds 0.55, or if it contains the expected WCAG SC number
and an element reference present in the code.  Issues matching no concept pool
entry are counted as FPs.

From the four counts, the following metrics are computed:

    Precision  =  TP / (TP + FP)
    Recall     =  TP / (TP + FN)
    F1 Score   =  2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

    MCC        =  (TP × TN − FP × FN)
                  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                  √[(TP+FP)(TP+FN)(TN+FP)(TN+FN)]

    Specificity =  TN / (TN + FP)
    NPV         =  TN / (TN + FN)

The Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) is used as a co-primary metric
alongside F1 because, as Chicco and Jurman (2020) demonstrate, MCC accounts for
all four cells of the confusion matrix and produces more informative comparisons
when class imbalances exist — which they do here, since most fixtures contain
more clean code elements (true negatives) than violation-bearing elements [26].

Six novel evaluation techniques supplement the standard confusion-matrix metrics.

Complexity Degradation Slope.  For each model, a linear regression is fitted
across mean F1 scores computed separately for low, medium, and high error
density fixtures.  The slope of this regression quantifies how much accuracy
degrades as fixture complexity increases.  Models with a steep negative slope are
less suitable for real-world deployment on dense, production-scale files.

Streaming Quality Checkpoints.  The streaming response is sampled at 25%, 50%,
75%, and 100% completion.  The cumulative TP and FP counts at each checkpoint
are recorded.  This measures whether important detections arrive early in the
stream (desirable for interactive IDE use) or are concentrated at the end of
long responses.

Pareto Frontier Analysis.  A scatter plot of F1 score against mean latency is
produced for all 18 models.  The performance Pareto frontier — models for which
no other model achieves higher F1 with lower latency — is identified.  This
provides the practical model-selection criterion for embedded IDE use, where
both quality and responsiveness are constrained.

Adversarial Blind-Spot Analysis.  The 25 adversarial HTML fixtures are scored
independently of the 16 core fixtures.  The adversarial set was stratified into
four difficulty bands (easy, medium, hard, expert) and eight defect categories
(button, form, image, heading, ARIA, link, table, complex).  Per-category recall
is computed to identify systematic blind spots — violation types that models of
all sizes consistently fail to detect.

Composite Score.  The composite score used for primary ranking is defined as:
    composite = 0.8 × F1 + 0.2 × speed_score
where speed_score is the model's reciprocal mean latency normalised to [0, 1]
across the cohort.  The 4:1 weighting reflects the user-study design's primary
interest in detection quality while acknowledging that latency is a practical
constraint in an IDE context.

Exact Match Accuracy.  For a subset of adversarial fixtures for which a single
exact canonical violation is expected, a secondary exact match score is computed:
a TP is only counted if the model identifies the precise element, WCAG criterion,
and severity without any accompanying FP.  This metric tests unambiguous,
confident detection under adversarial conditions.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.5.4  Establishing the Ground Truth
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The ground truth was manually authored by the researcher in ground-truth.ts and
validated against automated accessibility checking tools.  For each fixture, the
ground truth specifies:

    fixtureName   — the fixture file path
    language      — the file language (html, css, js, tsx)
    errorLevel    — the density tier (clean, low, medium, high)
    expectedCount — the count of individually identifiable violations
    conceptPool   — an array of canonical descriptor strings, one per violation

Concept pool entries follow a standardised format: failure description +
WCAG SC reference + element hint.  For example: "non-descriptive link text
'Click here' fails WCAG 2.4.6 — anchor element line 94".

Cross-Validation.  The validate-ground-truth.ts module exports each HTML fixture
to a DOM representation and runs axe-core and Pa11y against it.  The union of
violations reported by both automated tools is compared against the manually
authored concept pool.  Violations present in the automated output but absent
from the concept pool are flagged for researcher review; violations in the concept
pool absent from both automated tools (typically CSS or inter-element ARIA
reference issues) are retained with a researcher justification note.  This
cross-validation was completed before any benchmark run to ensure the ground
truth was internally consistent and defensible.

The adversarial fixture set was constructed separately to probe capabilities
beyond the core fixtures.  Each adversarial fixture targets a specific edge case
derived from common developer errors in accessibility implementation: non-obvious
interactive elements (e.g. div with onclick but no role="button"), ambiguous ARIA
reference targets, valid-looking but broken aria-controls chains, and visually
styled elements that appear accessible but fail programmatic accessibility checks.
Difficulty gradings (easy: n=4, medium: n=5, hard: n=16) were assigned by the
researcher after pilot testing confirmed that different model families placed the
boundaries in consistent locations.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.5.5  Subjective Workload Assessment (NASA-TLX)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The NASA Task Load Index (NASA-TLX) is a multidimensional subjective workload
instrument developed by Hart and Staveland (1988) [33] at the NASA Ames Research
Centre and subsequently validated across a wide range of human-computer
interaction tasks (Hart 2006) [34].  The instrument comprises six subscales:

    Mental Demand     — degree of mental and perceptual activity required
    Physical Demand   — degree of physical exertion required
    Temporal Demand   — degree of time pressure experienced
    Performance       — degree of success in accomplishing the task goals
    Effort            — degree of effort exerted to attain achieved performance
    Frustration Level — degree of irritation, stress, or annoyance experienced

In the present study, the NASA-TLX is applied in two distinct roles.

Role 1 — Computational At-Analysis Estimation.  The extension's NASA-TLX panel
(tlxPanel.ts) computes a predicted workload score from the issue report produced
by the analysis engine.  This estimation is based on the cognitive-load
prediction model described in knowledge-base/NASA-TLX/nasa-tlx-llm-prediction-
guide.md.  For each subscale, a base value is defined reflecting a zero-issue
baseline, and an incremental coefficient is applied per detected violation.
Violations classified as HIGH severity contribute larger increments than those
classified as MEDIUM or LOW.  Total predicted scores are capped at 100 per
subscale.

The validation of this prediction model was conducted by comparing predicted
TLX scores across a sequence of scenarios representing increasing violation
counts and severity.  The Pearson correlation between predicted scores and the
expected directional ordering was computed and found to be r = 1.0 across the
test scenarios, indicating that the prediction model is perfectly rank-consistent
with the direction of cognitive load increase.  It should be noted that the model
underestimates absolute TLX scores at high violation density — that is, the
predicted values are proportionally smaller than subjective pilot ratings for the
most complex fixtures — and calibration against empirical user-study data is
planned.

Role 2 — User Study Subjective Outcome Measure.  In the planned user study
(Section 3.4.4), participants will complete the NASA-TLX questionnaire after
each task condition.  The raw workload score (unweighted mean of all six
subscale responses) will be the primary subjective outcome measure.  The
unweighted raw TLX has been shown to perform comparably to the original weighted
TLXD variant in overall validity while reducing administration overhead (Hart
2006) [34].  Subscale scores will also be reported individually to identify
whether the extension primarily reduces Mental Demand, Temporal Demand, or
Frustration — the three subscales most theoretically linked to the information-
search task the extension is designed to replace.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.5.6  Statistical Correlation Plan
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The principal statistical analysis maps the extension's technical F1 score
(Section 3.5.3) to the mean NASA-TLX raw workload score (Section 3.5.5) across
participants in the user study.  The hypothesis under test is that a negative
correlation exists: as F1 increases, mean TLX score decreases, consistent with
H2 (Section 3.1.1).

A bivariate Pearson correlation coefficient will be computed between each
participant's per-condition TLX score and the F1 score achieved by the extension
on the fixtures presented in that condition.  Statistical significance will be
evaluated at α = 0.05.  Given the expected sample size of approximately 10–12
participants, a correlation of |r| ≥ 0.60 is required to achieve 80% statistical
power — a medium-to-large effect by Cohen's (1988) conventions.

Supplementary analyses will examine correlations at the subscale level to
determine which of the six TLX dimensions shows the strongest relationship with
detection accuracy.  This will enable a directional interpretation of the
workload data: if Mental Demand and Effort subscales correlate most strongly, the
extension is principally relieving the cognitive labour of manual violation
identification; if Temporal Demand correlates most strongly, the reduction is
primarily time-saving.

To account for order effects introduced by the counterbalanced A/B design, a
paired t-test will be conducted comparing TLX scores in the baseline condition
against TLX scores in the treatment condition across all participants.  If the
sessions are considered sufficiently independent (no learning carryover), a
Wilcoxon signed-rank test will be used as a non-parametric alternative given the
small sample size.

All statistical analyses will be conducted in Python using SciPy and pandas;
analysis scripts and raw data will be archived alongside the benchmark results
for full reproducibility.
