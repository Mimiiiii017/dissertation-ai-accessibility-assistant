Abstract

Abstract

The world of technology has witnessed significant strides, particularly with the
advent of image recognition. This empowers different computer platforms to interpret and analyze visual content from diverse media sources such as images
and videos. Pivotal to this evolution is the deployment of Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNNs), which are intricately designed to process visual data. With
their layered architecture, CNNs are adept at capturing hierarchical nuances from
images, systematically unearthing and distinguishing pivotal features and thus, discerning detailed visual patterns. Their transformative role is evident in a spectrum
of industries, ranging from healthcare and autonomous vehicles to digital security
and online retail. By autonomously understanding visual content, CNNs pave the
way for unprecedented innovations in the realm of computer vision.

This dissertation ventures into the domain of image recognition, with a specific
emphasis on the classification of diverse Italian dishes using Convolutional neural
Networks. The primary objective of this scholarly exploration is to conceptualize and develop robust, accurate, and reliable model tailored for distinguishing a
plethora of Italian dishes based on their unique visual characteristics. To achieve
this, the research engaged with several cutting-edge architectures including MobileNetV2, DenseNet, Xception, and InceptionV3, to evaluate their proficiency in
classifying the richness of Italian culinary artistry.

During the progression of this research, a series of challenges surfaced that influenced the outcomes. A paramount hurdle was the limitation of testing data,
which consequently led to a classification report and confusion matrix that did
not meet the anticipated benchmarks. This limitation posed significant constraints
on the model’s capability to validate its findings, thereby affecting its overall reliability and performance metrics. To address these setbacks, we embarked on a
iv

comprehensive investigative journey.

Experiments were initiated to enhance the model’s performance including the incorporation of hyperparameter tuning to ensure optimal learning rates and network
configurations. Additionally, data augmentation techniques were implemented, thereby
expanding the variability within the available dataset, and rendering the model
more resilient to visual variances within dishes. Furthermore, leveraging advanced
architectures such as MobileNetV2, DenseNet, Xception, and InceptionV3 provided a holistic view of each model’s strengths and potential areas of improvement in the classification of Italian dishes. Remarkably, amidst these challenges,
in terms of training accuracy and validation accuracy, the models used exhibited
commendable performances. This showcases the resilience and adaptability of the
deployed CNN architectures, reinforcing their potential in specialized classification
tasks.

Keywords: Convolutional Neural Networks, Image recognition, Italian dishes classification, Data augmentation, Hyperparameter tuning.

Chapter 1: Introduction

Image recognition remains a critical field of study, enabling computers to process and analyze visual content from images and videos. Its widespread applications span industries such as healthcare, security, and transportation. The literature
abounds with pivotal research papers that have heralded advancements in image
recognition. Yann LeCun’s seminal paper, ”Gradient-Based Learning Applied to
Document Recognition” from 1998, marked the introduction of the Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN) - a deep learning algorithm that has since become instrumental for modern image recognition systems [1]. Further compounding the
importance of CNNs, Krizhevsky et al., in their 2012 paper titled “ImageNet
Classification with Deep Convolutional Neural Networks”, showcased the efficacy
of CNNs on large-scale image recognition, presenting unmatched results on the
ImageNet dataset [2]. As the precision of image recognition technology evolves,
its transformative impact is palpable across sectors, catalyzing efficient data analysis, refined automation, and enriched user experiences.
Within the vast expanse of image recognition lies the specialized domain of
food image recognition, a challenge in computer vision with implications in recipe
recommendations and nutritional analysis. This dissertation zeroes in on the utilization of CNNs, specifically models such as MobileNetV2, InceptionV3, Xception, and DenseNet, for classifying various Italian pizza styles. CNNs have already proven their mettle in food image classification. For instance, Zhang et al.

2.1.1

What is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence refers to the field of developing computer systems and robots
which are capable of behaving in ways that they either mimic human actions or
go beyond what they are capable of. Ed Burns defines Artificial Intelligence as
“the simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, especially computer
systems.
Problems related to artificial intelligence provide information or trigger actions
automatically without human interference, and AI is used in various technologies
that are made use of today, including smart devices such as smart watches, smartphones, and many others [8]. Researcher John McCarthy said in his research that
artificial intelligence “is the science and engineering of making intelligent machines, especially intelligent computer programs. It is related to the similar task
of using computers to understand human intelligence, but AI does not have to
confine itself to methods that are biologically observable.” He adds that “Intelligence is the computational part of the ability to achieve goals in the world.
Varying kinds and degrees of intelligence occur in people, many animals, and
some machines.” [9].
Ed Burns states that “Specific algorithms of AI include expert systems, natural
language processing, speech recognition and machine vision.” [10] Nilsson states

2.1.2

History of Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence idea was first introduced in 1956 but wasn’t as popular
as it is nowadays. In the 1950s, research about AI took place and explored
topics like problem solving and symbolic methods, and in the 1960s, the US
Department of Defense took interest in AI and began training their computers
to mimic how humans reason. The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency
(DARPA) completed street mapping projects in the 1970s, and they also produced
intelligent personal assistants in 2003. These intelligent personal assistants were
developed long before Alexa or Cortana were household names.
This paved the way for the automation that we see in computers nowadays,
that include decision support systems and smart search systems designed to complement human abilities. AI, throughout the years has evolved to produce many
benefits in every industry. From the 1950s till the 1970s, early work with neural
networks was conducted and it stirred excitement for what is known as “thinking machines”. From the 1980s, Machine Learning became popular and nowadays deep learning has made a significant breakthrough in the world of AI. The
CEO of SAS software Jim Goodnight has had some positive remarks to make
about AI and stated “AI has been an integral part of SAS software for years.
Today we help customers in every industry capitalize on advancements in AI,
and we’ll continue embedding AI technologies like machine learning and deep

Dataset Distribution Analysis

In this dissertation, the total dataset was not leveraged all at once. Instead, different proportions of it were taken for both training and evaluation purposes.
Specifically, 30%, 40%, 70% and 100% of the dataset were utilized in separate
experiment runs. Here’s an elucidation of this unique approach and its rationale:
1. Understanding Model Behaviour with Limited Data – Starting with 30% of
the datasets helps in understanding how well all models perform when data
is scarce. This can be especially informative for scenarios where gathering
a lot of data might be expensive or time-consuming.
2. Scaling Up and Observing Trends – As we progress from 30% to 100%,
we can observe how the model’s performance changes as more data is introduced. This can help in identifying the ‘data saturation point’, which
refers to the point at which adding more data does not lead to significant
improvements in performance of the models, but further experimentations
need to be carried out to try and maintain positive results.
3. Resource Efficiency – By first training on smaller portions of the dataset
(such as 30% and 40%), we can quickly gauge the model’s potential performance without committing heavy computational resources. If the models
perform poorly, there might be underlying issues that need addressing before scaling up.
4. 4. Potential Overfitting with Smaller Datasets – With a smaller proportion
of the dataset, such as 30%, the models have less diverse data to learn
42

Chapter 4. Analysis of Results and Discussion

from. It may perform exceptionally well on this limited data, but might not
generalize well to new, unseen data. Observing this trend can shed light on
whether the model is overfitting.

4.1.1

Why different proportions?

1. 30% of the full dataset – This helps to understand the behaviours of the
different models used in data-limited scenarios. It’s a reality check where
if the model struggles in 30% of the full dataset, then it would be a clear
indication that more data or a change in the approach is important.
2. 40% of the full dataset – This serves as the next check after the initial
30%. At this stage, we evaluate the performance increments achieved with
an additional 10% of the dataset. It aids in understanding if there’s a substantial boost in accuracy or if it plateaus.
3. 70% of the full dataset – Close to utilizing the entire dataset but keeping
a portion of it untouched. This is an important stage where the performances of different models can be nearly optimal, yet there’s still room for
potential improvements by adding more data.
4. 100% of the full dataset – This is the final test, making use of all the
available data. Performance metrics here provide the upper limit of what
the model can achieve with the given data and architecture.
By employing this graduated approach, we can draw richer insights about the
dataset and the way different models behave, helping in making informed decisions for potential deployment or further research.

Comparative Analysis of the Convolutional Neural Networks

The central thrust of this research revolves around a rigorous examination of four
pivotal convolutional neural networks (CNN) models. Each chosen for its unique
design philosophy and a demonstrated track record in image classification, these
models collectively encapsulate the epitome of advancements in the deep learning
realm. This section unveils the key ideas and distinctive characteristics underlying
each model.

4.2.1

DenseNet

Originating from the core concept of Densely Connected Convolutional Networks,
DenseNet showcases an innovative architecture where each layer connects directly
to every subsequent layer, promoting enhanced feature propagation. Distinct from
conventional models where signals are procured from adjacent layers, DenseNet
taps into ‘shared knowledge’ across all preceding layers, offering a more integrated feature propagation. Its unique design not only offers solutions to challenges like the vanishing-gradient problem but also promotes efficient parameter
utilization. This positions DenseNet as an idea choice for intricate image classification tasks [70].

4.2.2

InceptionV3

An evolved iteration of GoogleNet, InceptionV3’s architecture is characterized by
its layered “network within network” approach.

Functionally, it capitalizes on

convolutional filters of different sizes operating in tandem, ensuring diverse scale

44

Chapter 4. Analysis of Results and Discussion

feature extraction. Further refinement comes from its integration of factorized and
asymmetric convolutions, streamlining computational demands. Its adaptability in
identifying patterns across various image scales and resolutions makes it particularly suited for datasets with a broad spectrum of image characteristics [71].

4.2.3

Xception

Inspired by the Inception framework, this model introduces a pivotal shift with
its depth wise separable convolutions. This deviation from the traditional convolutional methods means that the channels are processed individually before being
amalgamated. The underlying rationale is the separate mapping of spatial features
and channel correlations. This alternate approach of Xception provides insights
into conventional convolutional paradigms, offering potential advantages in model
efficiency and precision [72].

4.2.4

MobileNetV2

Crafted with mobile and embedded vision applications in mind, MobileNetV2
seamlessly blends efficiency with accuracy. Its architecture is punctuated by two
novel features: the inclusion of linear bottlenecks between layers and shortcut
connections over these bottlenecks. These design innovations coalesce to create
a streamlined model. Emblematic of the fine balance between computational efficiency and performance strength, MobileNetV2 emerges as a compelling model
for contexts with limited computational resources [73].

Discussion of Results

4.5.1

Challenges Faced

During the testing phase, several challenges arose that proved pivotal in shaping
the outcomes of the experiment. One of the primary challenges was the overfitting observed in models, particularly DenseNet, when trained with a smaller
fraction of the dataset. This overfitting raised concerns about the model’s genuine capability and its potential real-world effectiveness. Furthermore, there was
a noticeable discrepancy between high test accuracies and low class-specific metrics in several models, indicating potential evaluation inconsistencies or a lack of
balanced data representation.
Another significant limitation was the possible non-uniformity of the dataset
across its subsets. Since performance varied dramatically across different dataset
74

Chapter 4. Analysis of Results and Discussion

proportions, it’s crucial to consider if the subsets were truly representative of the
whole. If certain unique or challenging instances were disproportionately present
in one subset and not others, it could skew the model’s performance metrics for
that subset.

4.5.2

Challenges Faced

The observed results have profound implications for machine learning model selection, especially when dealing with varying amounts of data. MobileNetV2’s
consistent performance, irrespective of dataset proportion, underscores its robustness and adaptability. In contrast, models like DenseNet, despite their impressive
performance at smaller data proportions, may not be reliable indicators of realworld performance due to overfitting concerns.
Furthermore, the drastic drop-in class-specific metrics for InceptionV3 and Xception as the dataset size increased raises questions about their scalability and reliability. This suggests that while certain models might excel in constrained or
controlled environments, their efficiency might diminish when exposed to larger,
more diverse data.

4.5.3

Interpretation of Performance Variations across Test Sizes

The varying performance across different test sizes could be attributed to several
factors. Firstly, smaller dataset proportions might not capture the full complexity
or diversity of the entire dataset, leading to over-optimistic results. As the dataset
size increases, models are exposed to a broader array of data instances, some of
which might be more challenging or less represented in smaller subsets.

them more suited to specific data types or sizes. While MobileNetV2’s architecture seems well-adapted to varying data sizes, other models like InceptionV3 and
Xception might require more data tuning or specific architectural modifications to
handle larger datasets effectively.

4.5.4

Real World Applicability and Implications based on Varied Test Sizes’ Performance

The performance variations observed across different test sizes have profound implications for real-world applications. In practical scenarios, data availability can
be inconsistent; thus, choosing a model that performs well across different data
sizes, like MobileNetV2, can be more reliable and versatile.
However, if the end goal is a niche application with limited data, models
that show exceptional performance with smaller data proportions, like DenseNet,
might be considered. Still, caution should be exercised due to potential overfitting
concerns.
Additionally, the significant drop-in class-specific metrics for certain models
at larger dataset sizes suggests that these models may struggle to generalize in
real-world scenarios with diverse and complex data. Hence, rigorous testing and
validation in real-world contexts are crucial before deploying such models.
In conclusion, this discussion emphasizes the importance of rigorous testing
across varying data proportions and real-world scenarios to ensure the selection
of the most appropriate and reliable machine learning model.

Chapter 5: Conclusions and Recommendations

In the intricate dance of machine learning, particularly deep learning models, the
dimensions of data play a pivotal role in determining a model’s efficiency. This
study embarked on a mission to understand the performance variations of four
prominent models—MobileNetV2, InceptionV3, DenseNet, and Xception—across
different dataset sizes. Herein, we consolidate our findings, suggest model-specific
improvements, and reflect upon the broader implications of our discoveries.
MobileNetV2 has demonstrated a commendable consistency across diverse data
sizes, yet there remains an avenue for enhancements.

One potential area of

improvement lies in experimenting with various regularization techniques. Techniques such as Dropout or L1/L2 regularization might further buttress the model
against overfitting. Moreover, meticulous fine-tuning of its hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate, can likely optimize its performance even more.
InceptionV3 presented certain variances, especially in its class-specific metrics
when dealing with larger datasets. These observations hint at the potential advantages of introducing more layers or nodes to its architecture. Additionally, the
incorporation of batch normalization may offer a more stable learning trajectory.
Furthermore, the implementation of data augmentation techniques, encompassing
strategies like rotations and scaling, could be instrumental in bolstering its ability
to generalize across diverse inputs.
DenseNet, while showcasing promising initial results, also exhibited some po77

Chapter 5. Conclusions and Recommendations

tential pitfalls, notably in the form of overfitting. To navigate this challenge, the
implementation of strategies such as early stopping, complemented with model
checkpoints to capture the best iterations, might be beneficial. Moreover, considering DenseNet’s intricate, deep architecture, there’s merit in considering gradient
clipping as a solution to tackle any emerging gradient-related challenges.
Xception’s performance, particularly the discernible gap between its overall
test accuracy and specific class metrics, underscores the need for introspection.
One promising avenue could involve reevaluating the loss of functions employed.
Focal loss, for instance, could offer a more nuanced approach, especially for
challenging classes. Beyond this, a comprehensive optimization of the model’s
architecture might serve to elevate its overall performance.
MobileNetV2 has consistently demonstrated its mettle, especially when consistency across diverse dataset sizes is paramount. This adaptability makes it a
prime candidate for applications that grapple with evolving or unpredictable data
contexts. Conversely, when working within the confines of limited data availability, DenseNet appears to be the go-to. However, one should approach DenseNet
with an astute awareness of its susceptibility to overfitting. When the conversation shifts to striking a balance between computational thriftiness and robust
performance, MobileNetV2 shines yet again. Meanwhile, for endeavors that envision scaling to expansive datasets, a compelling argument can be made in favor
of both MobileNetV2 and a suitably tweaked Xception model.
Diving deep into the study’s core insights, it becomes apparent that the volume of data wields significant influence over model performance. This reaffirms
