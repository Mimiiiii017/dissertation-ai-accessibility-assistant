Abstract

Abstract

Voting is a process used by groups of people to make decisions, particularly in
relation to public, social, or political issues. The trustworthiness of democracy relies on the fairness and honesty of the election process itself. It is crucial for the
electoral system to be strong enough to prevent fraudulent practices and transparent and understandable enough for voters and candidates to accept the results. It
is not uncommon for elections to be manipulated to change the outcome. While
voting is highly important, traditional manual voting has several drawbacks, the
most notable being low voter participation. Electronic voting is a potential alternative that also eliminates the need for physical ballots or polling stations,
allowing authorised individuals to register and vote online using their own devices. The main advantage of these systems is that voters can cast their votes
from anywhere, without the need to visit specific locations. However, there are
concerns and perceived risks associated with electronic voting, such as security
and reliability issues, susceptibility to hacking and fraud, malicious software programming and insecure software transactions. By incorporating blockchain technology into electronic voting, the benefits of the method can be preserved while
reducing the likelihood of hacking or manipulation. The research concentrates on
utilising Ethereum’s decentralised platform and employing smart contracts written
in Solidity to establish a system that is both secure and adaptable. Additionally, two cryptographic techniques, namely ECDSA and the Paillier cryptosystem,
have been employed to enhance the security of votes and ensure the integrity of
data. For this research, a mixed-methods approach was employed. A total of
twelve participants, selected based on their age, occupation, and familiarity with
blockchain technology, were involved in the study. The participants were divided
into two groups: the control group and the experimental group, with each group
comprising six participants. The experimental group had access to the prototype
being tested, while the control group utilised a traditional paper ballot. Both beiv

fore and after the test, participants were asked to complete a questionnaire. The
initial questionnaire collected general information such as age, technological proficiency, and previous experience with various technologies. The post-test questionnaire focused on participants’ perceptions of safety and confidence during the

technology, with younger individuals demonstrating greater understanding and acceptance, while older participants exhibited more reservations or limited awareness
of its capabilities.

Chapter 1: Introduction

Voting is a method by which groups of people make decisions. These decisions
can be related to public, social, or political matters. Voting is also used to choose
from different plans of action or to determine who deserves a prize. Therefore,
voting can be seen as a process that allows a group of people to select from
various options. Most voting systems are based on the principle of majority rule
or plurality. For example, a candidate with a plurality receives more votes than
any other candidate in an election, although not necessarily the majority of all
votes cast. Elections provide the general public with the opportunity to choose
their representatives and express their opinions about the kind of government they
desire.
The integrity of democracy relies on the integrity of the election process itself. It is crucial for the electoral process to be strong enough to withstand
fraudulent tactics and transparent and understandable enough for voters and candidates to accept the outcomes. It is not surprising that there have been instances
where elections have been manipulated to alter the results. A well-designed voting system should meet conflicting requirements, whether it employs electronic
technology, traditional paper ballots, or mechanical devices.

To protect voters

from malicious candidates and prevent the buying of votes, it is essential for
voters to remain anonymous when casting their ballots. Having evidence of a
voter’s choice would enable a candidate to influence the outcome by purchasing

votes. The voting system should be accessible to all voters, regardless of age,
health, or disability. Ensuring accessibility for such a diverse population poses
significant engineering challenges. If implemented successfully alongside other security measures, electronic voting could be a significant improvement over the
current paper-based method. However, any issues with these aspects of a voting
system may lead to unreliable or inaccurate election results.
Voting is of utmost importance, but traditional manual voting comes with several drawbacks, with low voter turnout being the most notable [1]. There can be
various reasons for this, but a significant factor is the dislike for long queues,
which discourages many individuals from voting. Moreover, manual voting poses
challenges for voters who are unable to be physically present at the designated
location, and transportation difficulties can also impact elderly and disabled voters, further contributing to the problem. In a study conducted by researchers [2],
it was found that substantial delays in the election process are common due to
the time-consuming nature of hand counting each paper ballot. Despite governments’ efforts to promote voter awareness and emphasise the benefits of higher
voter participation in every election, these factors significantly impact voter interest [1].
Electronic voting, or e-voting, eliminates the need for physical ballot papers
and boxes, allowing eligible individuals to register and vote online using their
personal systems.

The key benefit of e-voting systems is that voters are not

required to visit specific voting centres as they can cast their votes from any
location. However, there are various drawbacks and concerns associated with e-

potential fraud, malicious software programming, insecure software transactions,
and the dominance of central authority in such systems [3].
Integrating blockchain technology with e-voting can help maintain the advantages of the methodology while reducing the risk of hacking or manipulation. By
implementing blockchain in the voting system, the fundamental voting process remains unchanged, but it has the potential to enhance voting participation [4].
This study demonstrates the potential of incorporating blockchain technology
into e-voting, making the process more sustainable by leveraging its immutable
and decentralised nature. It highlights that valuable and crucial data, particularly
when anonymity and incorruptibility are required, can be securely stored on the
blockchain. The features of blockchain offer a comprehensive solution for these
needs, as evidenced by multiple researchers. Furthermore, this study aims to significantly address the issues associated with traditional manual voting and electronic voting.

Purpose Statement

The purpose of this research is to address and mitigate the challenges associated with traditional paper-based voting and e-voting through the development of
a prototype utilising the Ethereum blockchain. The research focuses on leveraging Ethereum’s decentralised platform and smart contracts written in Solidity to
create a secure and flexible system. Additionally, the use of Ganache, a personal
blockchain, allows for development and testing without deploying the application

1.2

Hypothesis and Research Questions

Given the purpose of this study, it is hypothesised that blockchain technology
integrations with the ECDSA and Paillier cryptosystem can viably enhance the
conventional approach to voting systems in terms of security, versatility and processing which significantly alter the way they are currently being perceived. Following on this hypothesis, the following research questions were identified:

1. How can e-voting systems be more efficient than conventional manual voting systems in terms of processing and security through Blockchain integrations?
2. What effect does the Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA)
in conjunction with Paillier cryptography have on data integrity?
3. To which extent are e-voting systems instrumental and potentially hindering
in relation to civic engagement and voter turnout?
4. How does the blockchain network’s decentralisation minimise the chances
for vote tampering in e-voting systems?
5. How can blockchain e-voting systems retain lightweight and cost-effective
forms in view of sustainable means of e-voting?

2.1

Awareness of voting process in a variety of settings

The traditional and most common way of voting is by using paper ballots. Eligible voters are requested to physically go to voting centres, mark their choices
of candidates on ballot paper and deposit them in ballot boxes [2]. It is a very
simple methodology which has been used for a long time however, there are
many threats to this system [5]. Votes can be manipulated which may lead to
the election of the wrong head of government. Moreover, ballots can be miscounted, rejected or tampered by the third party responsible for vote counting.
One of the most significant drawbacks of this method is the low voter turnout,
which can be attributed to several factors. However, the most prominent reason is
that many individuals dislike waiting in long lines, which discourages them from
voting. Furthermore, manual voting for voters who are not physically present at
the designated place, as well as transportation challenges for elderly and disabled
voters, are key contributors to this problem. In a study conducted by [2], the au-

2.2

Recurrent issues commonly linked with voting in a variety of contexts

2.2.1

Issues associated with long queue times and economical aspect

Notably, long queue times is the most prominent issue associated with traditional
paper-based voting [9, 10]. The authors in [5] mention how most people do not
even go to voting at the voting sites due to the aggravation of waiting in line for
hours. Spending a lot of time in queue is impractical, thus some voters prefer
not to vote. Voting for school-based elections can also be very time consuming,
with students having to queue for a very long time to vote, as explained by the
authors in [6, 10].
Another major flaw of this voting system is the economical aspect in which a
large sum of money is used on paper ballots, voting activity, security and human
resources [5]. The authors in [11] point out that the paper ballots and boxes are
a waste for the environment after the elections are finished. Although smaller
scale voting such as school-based elections are made up of lesser candidates and
far less voters, the authors in [6] described how school elections for multiple
institutions are held every year. For each election, a large amount of paper ballots
and paper boxes are temporarily used, which have no use after the election is
over.

Security and privacy flaws that could potentially derive from an electronic voting approach

While e-voting has the potential to offer several benefits over the traditional paperbased voting methods, such as increased speed, convenience and accessibility, it

results for their own benefit. They progressed into their study by stating that although there are several variations of e-voting systems in use, very few of them
are trustworthy, transparent and have undergone extensive testing. The authors
in [12] researched the privacy flaws of e-voting by investigating the use of evoting in the Netherlands. The writers stated that the Dutch government heavily

2.4

Blockchain technology to address aforesaid issues with voting

2.4.1

The Decentralisation nature of the blockchain: What is it and what are the
benefits?

The decentralisation aspect of blockchain is one of its defining and crucial features, and it has far-reaching implications for the implementation of blockchain
technology in various areas, one of which is e-voting. The decentralised nature
of blockchain means that there is not one central authority that controls the network, thus promoting greater transparency and accountability [4].
The authors in [14] extensively discussed how conventional e-voting systems
store all their data in a central database controlled by a single entity, making it
susceptible to tampering and corruption as voters cannot confirm if their votes
were recorded correctly. On the other hand, in a blockchain-based e-voting system, the data is stored across multiple nodes in the network, each possessing
a copy of the complete database. This makes it nearly impossible for a single
entity to tamper with the data as any changes made to the database must be
agreed upon by most of the nodes in the network. This network is a transaction
log database which stores data in the form of blocks that are chained together
with the help of a cryptographic hash [15]. In order to access the data, all computers have to be accessed and the cryptographic hash will immediately alter if
the block is modified, signalling that the contents are altered [16]. As seen in

13

Chapter 2. Literature Review

Figure 2.1, each block contains the hash of the previous block which is used to
link the blocks into a chain.

Figure 2.1: Valid chain of three blocks

Each block has its own hash and knows the hash of the previous block. This
means that Block 3 points to Block 2 and Block 2 points to Block 1 (see Figure 2.1). The first block is called the Genesis block and is a special block which
does not point to any previous block, because it is the first one. If a block is
altered, then this will lead to a change in the hash of the block. If Block 2
is changed, Block 3 is invalidated, and the following blocks are no longer valid
(see Figure 2.2).

Figure 2.2: Invalid chain of three blocks

The authors in reference [4] highlight the importance of the decentralisation
feature of blockchain and its impact on electronic voting. They mention that decentralisation leads to increased transparency and accountability. Unlike traditional

Hashing and Digital Signatures in Blockchain

The use of hashing and digital signatures, which are critical to preserving the
integrity and security of the voting process, is one of the key features of the
blockchain technology. The authors in [17] described hashing as a process in
which a mathematical function is used to transform an input into a fixed-length
string, known as a hash value. This hash acts as a unique identifier for the input
and helps to secure the data stored in the blockchain. In e-voting, hashing is
utilised to ensure the accuracy and integrity of a voter’s ballot, by transforming
the vote into a hash value which can be verified later.
Meanwhile, the authors in [18] mention how digital signatures are used to
validate the authenticity of the data stored in the blockchain.

They work by

encrypting the data with a private key only known to the signer, and then can

Chapter 2. Literature Review

be decrypted by the recipient using the public key, confirming that the data was
signed by the person who claims to have signed it. In the context of e-voting,
digital signatures are used to verify that only authorized voters can cast ballots
and provide evidence that the vote was cast by the individual who claims to have
done so.
As per reference [17] and [18], the use of both hashing and digital signatures in blockchain-based e-voting systems offer several benefits over traditional
systems. Hashing protects the integrity of the data in the blockchain, as any
tampering will result in a different hash value. Digital signatures, on the other
hand, guarantee that only authorized users can vote and provide proof that the
vote was cast by the person who claims it.

Enabling public-key encryption via the Elliptic Curve Cryptography

In the realm of blockchain technology, a digital signature serves as an indispensable component that must be present, unless the hash function is deployed,
a blockchain cannot operate without utilising hashing functions or digital signatures. Therefore, any transaction conducted on the blockchain must be endorsed
with the sender’s electronic signature through their private key, or alternatively
hashed via hashing functions. Digital signatures constitute a fundamental building block in blockchains as they are utilised to authenticate the legitimacy of
transactions. In the event that someone submits a transaction, such as casting
a vote in an open ballot, they are required to verify their authorisation to ev-

ery node in the system. The blockchain nodes, or miners, who possess knowledge of the voter’s public key, will scrutinise the transaction’s conditions, and
authenticate the signature’s legitimacy. Once the verification process is successful,
the block holding the transaction will be set for finalisation by a validator. In
essence, to digitally sign a message means to provide authentication for its origin. Two of the most commonly employed digital signature algorithms include
RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) and ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography), of which
the latter is regarded as more superior [19].
ECC is a cryptographic method that utilises a public-key system based on an
elliptical curve. This method is known for producing smaller, faster, and more
efficient keys for encryption purposes. The keys are generated using the properties of the equation of the elliptical curve. ECC is classified as an asymmetric
cryptography technique. Despite having the same security level as RSA, ECC is
generally considered as a better option [20]. The Elliptic Curve Digital Signature
Algorithm (ECDSA) is a digital signature algorithm derived from ECC, which ensures the authenticity, integrity, non-repudiation, and origin of data. ECDSA can
operate as both a signature and a verification algorithm. Unlike RSA, ECDSA
employs shorter keys and requires less computational resources while retaining
robust security. The authors in [19] explained how a 256-bit ECDSA signature
provides the same level of security as a 3072-bit RSA signature.

Facilitating end-to-end verifiability through the integration of Paillier Homomorphic Encryption

Homomorphic encryption is an effective cryptographic method that allows specific computations to be conducted directly on ciphertext, ensuring that operations
carried out on the encrypted data yield the same results as those conducted on
the plaintext. This technique helps to safeguard data integrity by allowing others to manipulate the encrypted version of the data while making it impossible
for anyone to access or comprehend the decrypted values. By protecting privacy,
homomorphic encryption facilitates rapid access to encrypted data.
Paillier homomorphic encryption is a type of encryption that allows computations to be performed on the encrypted data without the need to decrypt it first.
This makes it a useful tool for e-voting systems, as it allows for votes to be securely counted without revealing the underlying vote. One of the key benefits of
Paillier homomorphic encryption in e-voting is that it allows for end-to-end verifiability, which is an important feature of any trustworthy e-voting system. With
end-to-end verifiability, voters can check that their votes were recorded correctly,
and election authorities can verify that the results of the election are accurate.
This encryption scheme contains a few weaknesses, such as the large size of
the encrypted text and the speed of encrypting plain data, as mentioned by the
authors in [21].
Although there are limitations to homomorphic encryption, numerous studies have been conducted to make it practically applicable, as shown in [22]
and [23]. Homomorphic encryption is highly suitable for e-voting systems be18

cause votes can be tallied in their encrypted form. This form of encryption allows data to be encrypted into hash numbers using a public key and stored in
a blockchain network. While homomorphic encryption provides privacy and security, the blockchain database ensures data integrity and transparency. Therefore,
homomorphic encryption and a blockchain network are complementary in creating
a fair e-voting system.

In this study, a software architecture called the three-tier architecture is being

the data tier to retrieve or store data, and with the presentation tier to provide
the necessary output. Lastly, the data tier, also known as the data access or
database tier, which is the bottom most layer of the architecture that deals with
the storage and retrieval of data.
In this research, both the voters and administrator use the presentation layer.
Initially, the administrator adds candidates which are sent to the logic tier for

blockchain.

blockchain.

questionnaire inquired about participants’ feelings of safety and confidence during the trial. A comprehensive understanding of the participants’ experiences and
viewpoints was achieved by collecting data from these surveys.

Limitations

Ethereum’s Network

The scalability challenge of the Ethereum network remains an ongoing issue. According to the researchers [26], as the number of participants and transactions
increases, congestion can occur, leading to delays and higher transaction costs.
This scalability problem can impact the performance of the e-voting system, particularly during times of high demand like large-scale elections. The reliability
of the Ethereum network relies on the underlying infrastructure and consensus
mechanism employed. Any disruptions or network outages can potentially affect
the availability and accessibility of the e-voting system, compromising its reliability and trustworthiness. Apart from Ethereum’s network, the internet itself is
another weakness, as described in [3], as performing e-voting through secure and
reliable internet requires significant security measures.

population. The results of this study were interpreted in accordance with the

5.2.1

Scaling the Blockchain Network

The authors in [26] presented a solution to scalability issues in blockchain-based
applications by implementing a technology known as sharding. Instead of having all nodes validate every transaction and block, sharding involves dividing the
data in a blockchain network into numerous segments or shards. This segmentation enables each shard to process transactions separately, resulting in greater
network concurrency and scalability. The researchers suggested applying sharding
to various blockchain platforms, including Bitcoin, Ethereum, Hyperledger Fabric,
Litecoin, Ripple, Dogecoin, among others. The goal was to divide the workload
across numerous shards, allowing for parallel processing and improving overall
system performance.
By adopting sharding, the researchers aimed to overcome the restrictions of
standard blockchain networks, which require all participating nodes to verify each
transaction and block, resulting in scalability issues. By permitting parallel processing of transactions over numerous shards, sharding has the potential to im50

Chapter 5. Conclusions and Recommendations

prove the throughput and scalability of blockchain networks. It was noted that
the effectiveness and specificity of sharding can vary based on the blockchain
platform and its design. Further research and development are required to investigate and optimize sharding solutions for various blockchain systems in order to
fully address scalability challenges.

analysis and increases the confidence of the study’s findings. It lessens the impact of individual differences and gives a more complete understanding of the
topic under investigation. Furthermore, a higher sample size can aid in identifying distinct subgroups within the population, allowing for more complex data
analysis and interpretation.
With more participants, it is possible to identify minor, but significant, effects
or variations in voters’ beliefs or behaviours. This improves the research findings’
