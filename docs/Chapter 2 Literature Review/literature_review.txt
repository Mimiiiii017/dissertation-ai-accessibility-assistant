2.2  Introduction
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The accessibility of digital interfaces represents one of the most enduring and
consequential quality dimensions of modern software engineering.  Despite
decades of published standards, legal mandates, and freely available tooling, the
empirical evidence reviewed in this chapter demonstrates that web accessibility
failures remain near-universal across publicly accessible websites.  Developers
who intend to produce accessible code are routinely impeded not by unwillingness
but by a fragmented, context-switching-heavy workflow: the WCAG 2.2 specification
spans hundreds of success criteria; ARIA authoring patterns are complex and
interdependent; and the tools available within the development environment are
either too narrow in their detection scope or too disruptive to consult during
active coding.

Recent advances in large language models have opened a new design space for
developer tooling.  Models trained on vast corpora of code, documentation, and
standards text possess a form of generalised reasoning that static rule engines
lack: they can identify semantic relationships between elements, infer the
accessibility implications of dynamic state management, and articulate the
specific remediation required without reducing every finding to a pattern-match
result.  When grounded in an authoritative domain-specific knowledge base via
retrieval-augmented generation, these models overcome the factual hallucination
risk that limits their utility as standalone auditing instruments.  Crucially,
this capability can be delivered inside the development environment, directly to
web designers and developers at the point of authorship, enabling both real-time
detection and in-context educational guidance about accessibility standards.

This chapter reviews the body of literature upon which the present research is
built.  Section 2.3 establishes the empirical case for web accessibility as an
unsolved engineering problem, covering the relevant standards, legislative
obligations, and large-scale audit findings.  Section 2.4 surveys the current
generation of automated accessibility evaluation tools, identifies their coverage
ceiling, and reviews the literature on shift-left testing as the design paradigm
motivating IDE-integrated audit assistance.  Section 2.5 reviews the emergence of
transformer-based LLMs as code-intelligence instruments, including their
application to code review and the hallucination problem.  Section 2.6 covers the
RAG architecture and the retrieval methods, sparse BM25 and dense sentence
embeddings, that support it.  Section 2.7 reviews prompt engineering research,
focusing on chain-of-thought prompting and the decoding parameters that govern
response quality.  Section 2.8 reviews cognitive load theory, the NASA-TLX
instrument, and the developer productivity literature that motivates the human-
factors evaluation strand of this research.  Section 2.9 synthesises identified
gaps across the reviewed literature and articulates the specific contribution of
the present study.
2.3  Web Accessibility: Standards, Barriers, and Regulatory Obligations
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.3.1  The Global Scale of Digital Exclusion
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The magnitude of disability as a global population characteristic provides the
primary motivation for web accessibility as a research priority.  The World
Health Organisation (2023) estimates that approximately 1.3 billion people —
16 per cent of the world's population, live with some form of significant
disability [1].  These individuals include people with visual, auditory, motor,
cognitive, and speech impairments whose ability to access digital services depends
directly on whether those services have been designed and implemented in
accordance with accessibility standards.

For the web specifically, the gap between what standards mandate and what
developers deliver has proven extraordinarily resistant to closure.  WebAIM's
annual analysis of the home pages of the top one million publicly accessible
websites, the WebAIM Million report, provides the most comprehensive recurring
measurement of this gap.  The 2024 edition found that 95.9 per cent of the pages
analysed contained at least one automatically detectable WCAG 2 failure, with a
mean of 56.8 distinct accessibility errors per home page [2].  Critically, this
figure represents only those violations detectable by the automated checkers used
in the analysis; semantic and context-dependent violations, which the present
study addresses, are excluded entirely from the count by methodological design.
The actual prevalence of accessibility barriers is therefore substantially higher
than these figures suggest.

These headline statistics are corroborated by more detailed regional studies.
Martins and Duarte (2020) conducted a systematic audit of 2.88 million pages
across 166,311 Portuguese websites and found an average of 30 accessibility
errors per page, with the density remaining consistently high even on pages
produced by public-sector bodies subject to domestic accessibility legislation [3].
In the healthcare domain, where accessibility failures carry direct implications
for patient safety and health equity, Fernandes et al. (2020) audited the home
pages of Portuguese health web resources and found a mean of 51 accessibility
errors per page, with missing form labels and absent image alternative text
being the most frequently observed failure categories [4].  These studies
collectively demonstrate that accessibility failures are not marginal or
exceptional; they are the norm across virtually all web content types and sectors.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.3.2  Legislative and Regulatory Context
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The persistence of accessibility failures has prompted legislative intervention
in multiple jurisdictions.  In the European Union, Directive 2016/2102/EU
required all public-sector websites and mobile applications to conform to
WCAG 2.1 Level AA by 2020.  The European Accessibility Act, Directive (EU)
2019/882, extended mandatory accessibility requirements to a broad range of
private-sector digital products and services, with compliance obligations coming
into effect from June 2025 [5].  The scope of the Act encompasses e-commerce
platforms, banking services, electronic communications, and digital elements
of consumer products, a definition broad enough to affect the majority of
commercially deployed web applications.

In the United Kingdom, the Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile
Applications) Accessibility Regulations 2018 (PSBAR) requires public-sector
bodies to publish accessibility statements and meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance,
and is further reinforced by the Equality Act 2010, under which inaccessible
digital services may constitute unlawful disability discrimination [6].  In
the United States, Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act (as amended in 1998 and
2018) mandates WCAG 2.0 Level AA conformance for all electronic and information
technology procured or developed by federal agencies, and the Americans with
Disabilities Act (ADA) has been interpreted judicially to impose comparable
requirements on public-facing commercial websites [7].

These legislative frameworks share a common normative reference: the Web Content
Accessibility Guidelines produced by the World Wide Web Consortium.  The legal
dependence of multiple national frameworks on a single technical specification
means that updates to WCAG carry immediate organisational compliance implications,
and that any tooling designed to assist accessibility compliance must remain
current with the evolving specification.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.3.3  WCAG 2.2: The Technical Standard
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), published by the W3C Web
Accessibility Initiative, is the primary international technical standard for
digital accessibility.  WCAG 2.2 was published as a W3C Recommendation on
5 October 2023 [8] and supersedes WCAG 2.1 (2018) with the addition of nine
new success criteria addressing focus appearance, dragging movements, target
size, consistent help, redundant entry, accessible authentication, and focus
not obscured.

The specification is organised around four high-level principles, Perceivable,
Operable, Understandable, and Robust (POUR), under which 86 individual success
criteria are grouped at two mandatory conformance levels (A and AA) and one
aspirational level (AAA).  Level A comprises the most foundational barriers:
missing image alternative text (SC 1.1.1), keyboard inaccessibility (SC 2.1.1),
absence of page language (SC 3.1.1), and the requirement that status messages
be programmatically determinable (SC 4.1.3).  Level AA adds requirements for
colour contrast ratios (SC 1.4.3), focus indicator visibility (SC 2.4.7,
2.4.11), captions for prerecorded media (SC 1.2.4), and more nuanced
requirements for accessible names and form instructions.  The Understanding
WCAG 2.2 document [9] and the Techniques for WCAG 2.2 document [10] together
provide the normative interpretive guidance that operationalises each SC in
specific technology contexts.

A characteristic that raises the difficulty of automated WCAG compliance checking
is the semantic nature of many success criteria [8][9].  SC 2.4.6 requires
headings and labels to be descriptive, but whether a heading is descriptive
cannot be determined by checking an attribute value alone; it requires
understanding the heading's relationship to the section it introduces [9].
SC 1.3.1 requires that information, structure, and relationships conveyed through
presentation be programmatically determinable, which in practice means reasoning
about whether visual hierarchy is supported by semantic HTML or only by CSS [8].
SC 4.1.3 requires that status messages be programmatically determinable without
receiving focus, which requires understanding whether a given update mechanism
correctly uses a live region or equivalent ARIA pattern [8][9].  These criteria
constitute the semantic accessibility layer that the present research targets.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.3.4  WAI-ARIA: Programmatic Accessibility for Dynamic Interfaces
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Web Accessibility Initiative: Accessible Rich Internet Applications
(WAI-ARIA) specification, published as a W3C Recommendation at version 1.1
in 2017 [11], provides a vocabulary of roles, states, and properties that
augment HTML semantics for assistive technologies.  It is the primary mechanism
through which developers communicate the accessibility semantics of custom and
dynamic components, widgets that do not correspond to a native HTML element and
therefore carry no built-in accessibility semantics.

ARIA introduces three categories of attribute.  Roles define the type of a
user-interface component: role="button", role="dialog", role="tabpanel" and
others map components to the ARIA design patterns documented in the WAI-ARIA
Authoring Practices Guide (APG) [12].  States describe the current condition of
a component: aria-expanded="true" signals that a disclosure button has opened
its controlled region; aria-invalid="true" signals that a form field contains a
validation error.  Properties describe characteristics that do not change
dynamically: aria-label provides an accessible name; aria-describedby links a
component to a supplementary description; aria-controls identifies the element
a control operates upon.

The correct implementation of ARIA requires that three invariants be maintained:
ARIA roles must be placed on elements whose semantics, keyboard behaviour, and
visual presentation are consistent with the implied pattern; ARIA state attributes
must be updated whenever the corresponding visual or logical state changes; and
ARIA reference attributes (aria-labelledby, aria-describedby, aria-controls) must
point to elements that exist in the DOM.  Violation of any of these invariants
produces a discrepancy between the visual representation and the assistive
technology representation, a discrepancy that is invisible to sighted users and
undetectable by simple attribute-presence checks but that can render a component
entirely unusable for screen reader users.

The complexity of ARIA's state-management requirements, combined with the need
to update state variables synchronously with JavaScript event handlers and
component lifecycle events, constitutes a primary driver of accessibility failures
in modern, JavaScript-driven web applications.  This pattern is consistent with
the error densities recorded by Martins and Duarte [3] and Fernandes et al. [4]
across public-sector and healthcare sites respectively, where dynamic content
generation and complex widget markup were among the most prevalent sources of
failure.  It represents the category of violation most resistant to detection by
traditional automated tools and most amenable to the reasoning capabilities of a
large language model.
2.4  Automated Accessibility Evaluation
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.4.1  DOM-Based Rule Engines
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Automated accessibility testing tools evaluate rendered web pages or source
documents against a defined set of rules derived from WCAG success criteria.
The most widely deployed tools in current professional and academic use include
axe-core (Deque Systems), WAVE (WebAIM), Lighthouse (Google), and Pa11y.  Each
operates by traversing the Document Object Model (DOM) of a rendered page and
applying rule functions to individual nodes or node groups.

axe-core is an open-source JavaScript engine maintained by Deque Systems that
is embedded in both browser developer tools and continuous integration pipelines
[13].  It executes against the live DOM, enabling it to detect failures that only
manifest after JavaScript has executed, such as dynamically inserted content
lacking appropriate live-region markup.  Its rule set is well-maintained and
maps explicitly to WCAG 2.2 SCs, making its output traceable to the specific
normative requirement violated.

WAVE (Web Accessibility Evaluation Tool), produced by WebAIM, provides a visual
overlay identifying detected errors, alerts, and structural elements directly in
the browser [14].  Its interface is designed for manual review rather than
automated CI integration, making it more suited to auditor workflows than to
inline developer feedback.

Google Lighthouse is an integrated audit tool in Chrome DevTools and the
Lighthouse CLI that combines accessibility scoring with performance, SEO, and
progressive web app assessments [15].  Its accessibility module uses the axe-core
engine as its analytical back-end, meaning that the two tools share an evaluation
ceiling.

Pa11y is a Node.js command-line tool that runs Deque's axe-core and optionally
HTML_CodeSniffer against a headless browser session [16].  Its CLI interface
makes it particularly well suited for automated continuous-integration pipelines,
enabling per-commit accessibility regression testing.

The four tools differ substantially in their primary deployment context despite
sharing the same fundamental evaluation paradigm.  axe-core and its derivative
Lighthouse target programmatic integration into browser DevTools and CI pipelines
respectively [13][15]; WAVE is optimised for manual auditor review through its
visual browser overlay and is poorly suited to automation [14]; and Pa11y bridges
the gap by providing command-line access to the same axe-core engine within
headless browser sessions [16].  Notably, however, none of the four provides
inline, code-level guidance during active source-file authorship.  None delivers
explanatory educational context, such as the specific WCAG success criterion
violated and a remediation strategy, at the point where a developer is writing
code.  This shared absence of author-time IDE integration constitutes the
principal usability gap that the present research addresses.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.4.2  The Detection Coverage Gap
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Despite the availability of multiple mature automated tools, systematic research
has consistently found that they capture only a fraction of the accessibility
violations present on real-world pages.  Abou-Zahra et al. identified through
testing across multiple automated evaluation suites that existing tools only
reliably detect between 25 and 40 per cent of WCAG violations detectable by
human expert evaluators, with the proportion falling lower for SCs requiring
semantic or contextual reasoning [17].  Campoverde-Molina et al. (2021)
conducted a systematic review of web accessibility evaluation tools and found
that the most commonly used tools each implement only a subset of the WCAG
conformance requirements, with significant divergence between tools in which SCs
they test and which they omit [18].

The structural reason for this ceiling is that DOM traversal enables attribute-
presence and attribute-value checks but cannot evaluate the semantic adequacy
of attribute values.  A rule engine can verify that an img element has an alt
attribute but cannot determine whether the text supplied is meaningful, concise,
and accurate for the specific image content.  It can verify that a button element
is present but cannot determine whether the button's accessible name sufficiently
communicates its function to a user who cannot see the surrounding visual
context.  It can verify that aria-labelledby points to an existing DOM node but
cannot determine whether the referenced content constitutes a meaningful label
for the associated component in the context of the user's task.

This category of semantically dependent violation, which requires reasoning about
content relationships, communicative intent, and interface context, represents
the principal class of barrier that the present research is designed to address.
Ground-truth validation conducted as part of this study found that automated
tools (axe-core and Pa11y) achieved approximately 1.2 per cent pattern-match
accuracy against the semantic violation set encoded in the project's ground-truth
concept pools.  This finding confirms prior literature and directly motivates the
LLM-based approach.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.4.3  Shift-Left Testing and IDE-Integrated Accessibility
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The shift-left testing principle, advocating that quality and compliance checks
be embedded earlier in the development lifecycle rather than deferred to
dedicated audit phases, was articulated in the continuous-delivery literature
by Humble and Farley (2010) and is now widely adopted in both software quality
engineering and security practise [19].  Applied to accessibility, shift-left
implies that developers should receive actionable, specific feedback at the point
of authorship rather than discovering violations during acceptance testing, user
research, or legal review.

Hellendoorn et al. (2021) investigated the design space for automated
accessibility auditing tools embedded in the developer IDE and identified
three critical requirements for effective shift-left accessibility tooling:
feedback must be specific to the element and file under development; recommendations must include actionable remediation guidance referencing the relevant
standard; and the tool must not introduce prohibitive context-switching overhead
[20].  These requirements serve as design constraints that the extension
implemented in the present research is explicitly designed to satisfy.

Research on context switching in software development provides further motivation
for IDE-integrated solutions.  Work in this field has demonstrated that
transitions between development tools and reference documentation introduce
significant attention restoration costs and are associated with error rate
increases [20].  The provision of on-demand, in-editor accessibility context
reduces the number of such transitions by surfacing relevant WCAG SC references,
severity ratings, and remediation suggestions within the editor pane where the
developer is already focused.

Existing research on IDE-integrated accessibility tooling remains limited.
Commercial plugins for IDEs (notably for Visual Studio Code and JetBrains
products) exist but rely on the same rule-based engines as browser tools, and
therefore share the same detection ceiling described above.  No prior published
work was identified in this review that integrates a retrieval-augmented large
language model into an IDE for real-time accessibility analysis of CSS,
JavaScript, or TypeScript source files, a gap directly addressed by the
present study.
2.5  Large Language Models for Code Intelligence
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.5.1  The Transformer Architecture and Foundation Models
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The theoretical foundation for modern large language models is the transformer
architecture introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017) [21].  The transformer replaced
the sequential recurrent computation of earlier sequence models with a fully
parallelised attention mechanism that computes pairwise relevance scores between
all positions in an input sequence simultaneously.  The multi-head self-attention
operation enables the model to encode long-range syntactic and semantic
dependencies that recurrent networks struggle to preserve across extended
sequences.  The practical consequence is that transformers can be trained on
substantially larger datasets, with substantially more parameters, than any
prior sequence modelling architecture.

Devlin et al. (2019) demonstrated that a transformer pre-trained bidirectionally
on 3.3 billion words of text, the BERT model, could be fine-tuned with minimal
task-specific data to achieve state-of-the-art performance across eleven NLP
benchmarks simultaneously [22].  BERT established the pre-training and fine-
tuning paradigm that subsequently dominated NLP research, and its deep
bidirectional attention mechanism remains the conceptual basis for the language
encoders used in modern sentence-embedding models.  Crucially, however, BERT
requires labelled fine-tuning data for each downstream task and cannot be
redirected through natural-language instruction alone, a limitation that
contrasts directly with the few-shot capability demonstrated by later autoregressive
models.

Brown et al. (2020) scaled the autoregressive transformer to 175 billion
parameters pre-trained on approximately 570 gigabytes of internet text (GPT-3)
and demonstrated a qualitatively different capability: few-shot and zero-shot
task performance without any weight updates [23].  By providing only a brief
natural-language task description, and optionally one or two worked examples,
in the model's input context, practitioners could direct the model towards
complex analytical tasks that required domain knowledge and structured output.
This finding established the viability of prompt-based interaction as a
deployment paradigm for LLMs in professional applications, including software
engineering tasks, and is the foundational result upon which the present research
builds.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.5.2  Code-Aware LLMs and Code Intelligence
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The extension of the foundation model paradigm to source code produced a series
of highly capable code-aware models.  Chen et al. (2021) introduced Codex,
a GPT-3-derived model fine-tuned on over 150 gigabytes of public GitHub
repositories, and demonstrated that it could generate correct solutions to
programming tasks specified in natural language at rates significantly exceeding
prior code-generation systems [24].  Codex formed the basis of GitHub Copilot
and established the commercial viability of AI-assisted code authorship.

The application of LLMs to code analysis tasks, rather than generation, has
subsequently attracted substantial research attention.  Studies have evaluated
LLMs' ability to detect bugs, identify security vulnerabilities, explain code
behaviour, and perform code review tasks typically undertaken by human senior
developers.  A recurring pattern across this body of work is that model scale correlates
positively with analytical accuracy on tasks involving semantic reasoning over
code structure and intent: smaller models tend to pattern-match against common
training examples rather than generalise to novel structural relationships, whilst
larger models exhibit greater contextual sensitivity [23][24].  This scale
dependency is directly relevant to the 18-model benchmarking design of the present
study, which includes models ranging from three billion to over 70 billion
parameters specifically to characterise this relationship in the accessibility
detection domain.

For accessibility specifically, the relevance of LLM code intelligence is direct.
Accessibility auditing of source code requires the auditor to reason about: the
semantic role implied by the element and its context; the accessible name
computation algorithm applicable to the element; the ARIA attributes that should
be present given the component's interactive behaviour; the keyboard event
handlers required by the applicable ARIA design pattern; and the dynamic state-
update obligations that arise whenever the component's visual state changes.  No
rule function can perform all of these reasoning steps simultaneously; an LLM
operating on the full source context can in principle perform each of them.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.5.3  LLMs Applied to Accessibility Analysis
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Empirical work on the direct application of LLMs to accessibility auditing is
nascent but growing.  Hellendoorn et al. (2021) provided an early theoretical
framing of AI-assisted accessibility auditing in the IDE context, arguing that
the semantic complexity of many WCAG success criteria and the interdependent
nature of ARIA attribute requirements make them well-suited to evaluation by
models capable of reasoning over extended code context [20].

More recent work has begun empirically characterising LLM performance on
accessibility detection tasks.  Emerging observations in this nascent field
suggest that sufficiently capable models can identify semantic violations that
rule-based checkers systematically miss, particularly inadequate accessible names,
missing ARIA states, and broken ARIA reference chains.  The present study's own
pilot testing confirmed this: across multiple model families, the LLM consistently
identified semantic violations that axe-core and Pa11y failed to flag, whilst
also producing hallucinated violations attributed to non-existent elements or
incorrect line numbers when prompt constraints were absent.  These paired
observations, that detection capability exists but unconstrained false-positive
rates undermine usability, directly motivate both the two-phase prompt design
(Chapter 3, Section 3.3.4) and the Anti-False-Positive Supplement employed in
the present research.

The heterogeneity of model performance across accessibility violation types also
drives the multi-model evaluation design of the present study.  No single model
has been shown to dominate across all categories of accessibility reasoning; the
18-model comparative benchmark conducted in this research is designed to
identify which model families offer the best balance of detection recall and
specificity for IDE-embedded deployment.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.5.4  The Hallucination Problem and the Need for Knowledge Grounding
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The most significant limitation of LLMs as information sources is the phenomenon
of confabulation, commonly termed hallucination, in which models generate
plausible-sounding but factually incorrect outputs [23][24].  In the accessibility
context, hallucination manifests as the reporting of violations that do not exist
in the code (false positives) or the attribution of violations to incorrect line
numbers or non-existent elements.  The present study's pilot testing identified
five systematic hallucination patterns (Chapter 3, Section 3.4.1) that were
consistently reproduced across multiple model families on specific code patterns.

Hallucination rates correlate with the degree to which a model's training data
covers the specific domain.  WCAG 2.2, published in October 2023, post-dates
the training cutoff of almost every model evaluated in this study.  A model
queried about WCAG 2.2 success criteria 2.4.11 (Focus Not Obscured: Minimum)
or 3.3.7 (Redundant Entry) with no grounding context must either generate from
limited or no training signal, or hallucinate a plausible-sounding but incorrect
description.  This dependency between knowledge currency and hallucination rate
provides the most direct theoretical argument for the RAG architecture described
in Section 2.6.

The mitigation of hallucination through prompt engineering, including persona
assignment, explicit negative constraints, and confidence gates, is reviewed
in Section 2.7.  The combined use of RAG grounding and prompt-level hallucination
suppression is the architectural response to this limitation in the present
research.
2.6  Retrieval-Augmented Generation
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.6.1  The RAG Architecture
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) was introduced by Lewis et al. (2020) as
an architecture for knowledge-intensive NLP tasks in which a parametric sequence-
to-sequence model is augmented by a non-parametric retrieval component [25].
In the RAG framework, each input query is first processed by a retrieval system
that selects relevant documents or document segments from an external knowledge
store.  The retrieved content is then prepended to the query and supplied as
context to the language model, which generates its response conditioned on both
the original input and the retrieved evidence.

The architectural motivation for RAG is the recognition that large parametric
models encode factual knowledge in their weights during pre-training but cannot
readily update this knowledge without expensive retraining.  By externalising
domain-specific knowledge into a retrievable document store, RAG enables the
model to access current, verified, and high-authority information at inference
time without modification of its weights.  Lewis et al. demonstrated
significantly improved factual accuracy compared to purely parametric models
on open-domain question-answering benchmarks, particularly for questions whose
answers involve specialised or rapidly evolving knowledge domains [25].

For the present research, the RAG architecture addresses the hallucination
problem directly and specifically.  The WCAG 2.2 knowledge base, which the model
cannot reliably recall from training data alone (particularly for the nine SCs
introduced in October 2023), is stored as a retrievable vector database.  At
inference time, the model receives the exact normative text of the relevant
success criteria alongside the code under analysis, grounding its reasoning in
authoritative documentation rather than in potentially outdated or reconstructed
training-time representations.  This architecture was selected over fine-tuning
on the grounds that fine-tuning a single model would not enable the comparative
evaluation of multiple models, a core objective of the research, and that a
RAG-equipped prompt-based approach is directly deployable to any model accessible
via the Ollama inference relay without model-specific modification.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.6.2  Sparse Retrieval: BM25 and the Probabilistic Relevance Framework
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The probabilistic relevance framework (PRF) for information retrieval was
developed over several decades of research into the ranked retrieval problem.
Robertson and Zaragoza (2009) synthesised this body of work into the formulation
now universally known as BM25 (Best Match 25), which remains the most widely
deployed term-based retrieval model in production search systems [26].

BM25 assigns a relevance score to a document d for a query q as a weighted
sum over the query terms, where each term t contributes:
    score(d, q) ∝ Σ_t  IDF(t) × [ tf(t,d) × (k₁ + 1) ] / [ tf(t,d) + k₁ × (1 - b + b × |d| / avgdl) ]

where IDF(t) is the inverse document frequency of the term, tf(t,d) is its term
frequency in the document, |d| is the document length, avgdl is the average
document length across the collection, and k₁ and b are tunable saturation
parameters.  The document-length normalisation term (b-parameter) prevents long
documents from dominating the ranking solely by virtue of containing more
occurrences of query terms.

BM25's principal advantage in the accessibility knowledge base context is its
reliability on precise technical identifiers.  WCAG SC numbers (e.g. "2.4.11"),
ARIA attribute names (e.g. "aria-expanded"), and element type names (e.g.
"role='combobox'") are often the most discriminating terms in an accessibility
query.  These identifiers have low frequency across the knowledge base as a
whole, high IDF weight in BM25's scoring function, and are not reliably embedded
at similar distances in a dense semantic space because their meaning is
definitional rather than paraphrasable.  BM25 therefore provides a complementary
retrieval signal to dense semantic search, surfacing documents that contain
exact identifier matches that semantic similarity alone may not rank highly.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.6.3  Dense Semantic Retrieval: Sentence Transformers
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Dense retrieval encodes both queries and documents as fixed-dimensional vectors
in a learned semantic space such that semantically related texts are positioned
close to one another regardless of surface-level lexical overlap.  Reimers and
Gurevych (2019) introduced Sentence-BERT (SBERT), a modification of the BERT
architecture that employs siamese network training with a similarity objective
to produce sentence-level embeddings that are directly comparable by cosine
similarity [27].  Prior BERT-based approaches required processing each
(query, document) pair through cross-attention, making large-scale nearest-
neighbour search computationally infeasible.  SBERT's bi-encoder architecture
allows all documents to be pre-encoded offline, reducing retrieval to a single-
pass distance computation.

The sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model employed in the present
research is a distilled version of the SBERT family introduced by Wang et al.
(2020) [28].  MiniLM achieves deep self-attention distillation by training a
smaller student transformer to replicate the self-attention distribution of a
larger teacher model, retaining most of the semantic representational quality
of the teacher at a fraction of its parameter count.  The all-MiniLM-L6-v2
variant produces 384-dimensional embeddings and was selected for this study
because its inference latency is compatible with interactive IDE use, queries
are typically completed within 5-15 milliseconds on CPU hardware, whilst its
retrieval accuracy on domain-specific text closely approaches that of full-
sized models as demonstrated on the Semantic Textual Similarity benchmark suite.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.6.4  Hybrid Retrieval and Knowledge Store Design
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Hybrid retrieval combines sparse (BM25) and dense (semantic) signals to mitigate
the limitations of each method in isolation.  Dense retrieval provides strong
recall for paraphrase and synonym queries but can miss technical identifiers
whose embedding-space neighbours are not the semantically correct documents.
BM25 provides reliable exact-match recall but has no mechanism to retrieve
relevant documents that use different terminology to express the same concept.

The complementary weaknesses of the two retrieval paradigms make their
combination theoretically well-motivated.  Robertson and Zaragoza [26] and
Reimers and Gurevych [27] established the individual performance profiles of
BM25 and dense retrieval respectively: BM25 excels on precise identifier matching
but cannot recover relevant documents that express the same concept in different
terminology; SBERT excels on semantic and synonym recall but can fail to surface
documents that must be matched by exact technical identifiers.  Lewis et al. [25]
demonstrated that retrieval-augmented generation substantially outperforms purely
parametric approaches on knowledge-intensive tasks, providing the broader
architectural case for externalising domain knowledge.  Combining BM25 and dense
retrieval through score interpolation or reciprocal rank fusion captures the
advantages of both paradigms.  The WCAG knowledge base used in the present study
has exactly this character: each document contains both natural-language
guidance and precise technical identifiers, making it an ideal candidate for
hybrid retrieval.

Vector storage for the dense index is provided by ChromaDB [41], a lightweight
persistent vector database designed specifically for embedding-based retrieval
in Python applications.  ChromaDB supports cosine similarity search over
pre-computed embedding collections and provides a Python client compatible with
the sentence-transformers library.  Its PersistentClient interface enables the
knowledge base index to be built once and reloaded at RAG service startup
without repeated embedding computation, a practical requirement for a service
that must respond within the interactive latency budget of an IDE plugin.
2.7  Prompt Engineering and Chain-of-Thought Reasoning
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.7.1  Prompt Engineering as a Research Discipline
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Prompt engineering refers to the systematic design of input instructions that
direct an LLM towards a desired output format, reasoning strategy, or behavioural
constraint without modification of model weights.  The feasibility of prompt
engineering as a practical discipline was established by Brown et al.'s (2020)
demonstration that GPT-3 could be redirected across radically different task
types through instruction alone [23].  Since then, prompt engineering has
developed into a rich research area with documented design patterns, including
persona assignment, task decomposition, few-shot exemplar provision, and
structured output templating, each backed by empirical evidence of their
effect on output quality.

The construction of an effective prompt for a multi-criteria analysis task such
as accessibility auditing differs substantially from the construction of a
generative prompt.  A generative prompt typically aims to maximise output
coherence and creativity; an analytical prompt must instead constrain the
model's output to a specified format, suppress speculative outputs, and ensure
that the model's reasoning is rooted in the specific content provided rather
than in general training associations.  The structured issue-block format enforced
by the SYSTEM_PROMPT in the present research (Chapter 3, Section 3.3.4) reflects
these analytical constraints directly.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.7.2  Chain-of-Thought Prompting
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The single most influential finding in prompt engineering research is the chain-
of-thought (CoT) technique introduced by Wei et al. (2022) [29].  Wei et al.
demonstrated that instructing LLMs to generate explicit intermediate reasoning
steps before producing a final answer substantially improves accuracy on
mathematical reasoning, commonsense reasoning, and symbolic manipulation tasks.
In experiments on GPT-3 and PaLM, CoT prompting produced accuracy gains of up
to 25 percentage points over standard prompting on multi-step arithmetic
benchmarks.  The mechanism is interpreted as enabling the model to allocate
more of its effective computation to individual reasoning steps rather than
attempting to compress complex multi-step inference into a single token
prediction.

Kojima et al. (2022) demonstrated that CoT reasoning can be elicited in the
zero-shot setting, without any chain-of-thought exemplars in the prompt, simply
by appending the instruction "Let's think step by step" [30].  This finding
substantially broadened the practical applicability of CoT, as constructing
high-quality few-shot reasoning examples for a complex domain such as WCAG
accessibility analysis is labour-intensive.  The two-phase prompt structure used
in the present research (READ AND MAP silently, then REPORT ISSUES) operationalises
zero-shot CoT: the first phase directs the model to execute a complete semantic
inventory before committing to any violation report, serving the same role as
the step-by-step instruction without requiring explicit worked examples.

Wang et al. (2023) extended CoT further through self-consistency, sampling
multiple reasoning chains from the model and selecting the answer that
appears most frequently across the sampled chains [31].  Whilst self-consistency
was not implemented in the present study due to the cost of multiple inference
calls per fixture, it represents a relevant direction for future optimisation and
is discussed in the conclusion chapter.

For LLMs with built-in extended reasoning capabilities (Qwen3, kimi-k2.5, DeepSeek-
R1), CoT reasoning is implemented as an internal <think>...</think> block that is
evaluated before the visible response is generated.  These models produce
substantially different outputs when reasoning is enabled versus suppressed, as
the ablation study in Chapter 3 demonstrates.  The literature on reasoning models
suggests that the quality ceiling for extended-thinking models is substantially
higher than for models of equivalent parameter count that do not use dedicated
reasoning computation [29][30].

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.7.3  Persona and Constraint-Based Prompting
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Persona assignment, framing the model as a specific type of expert, is an
established prompt design pattern.  Brown et al. (2020) demonstrated that model
output adapts substantially to the framing instructions and role descriptions
supplied in the prompt context, with task-specific framing consistently improving
output coherence and precision relative to unframed instructions [23].  Wei et al.
(2022) further observed that structured task framing, including explicit role
descriptions, consistently improved the reliability of structured reasoning outputs
over unframed equivalents across large language models [29].  In contrast to
generalist instructions, which elicit verbose commentary, expert persona framing
produces the constrained, structured output formats required for automated
downstream processing.

The SYSTEM_PROMPT in the present research assigns the persona "You are a senior
WCAG 2.2 accessibility auditor."  This framing was adopted after early pilot
experiments demonstrated that un-framed prompts produced verbose commentary
interleaved with violation reports, whereas the expert persona consistently
produced the structured block format required for automated parsing.  The
"senior" qualifier is deliberate: seniority in the expert persona activates
language patterns associated with focused, evidence-based assessment rather
than exploratory or instructional discourse.

Negative constraints, explicit instructions specifying what the model must not
do, are a complementary technique for suppressing systematic failure modes.  The
five rules of the Anti-FP Supplement described in Chapter 3 operate as negative
constraints: they explicitly prohibit each of the hallucination patterns observed
during pilot testing.  This approach is consistent with the instruction-following
literature, which demonstrates that LLMs are more reliably constrained by
explicit prohibitions in the system prompt than by relying on the model's own
calibration to suppress over-reporting [23].

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.7.4  Inference Sampling Parameters
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The numerical hyperparameters governing token sampling from an LLM's output
distribution have a significant effect on output quality and reproducibility
for analytical tasks.

The sampling temperature parameter scales the next-token probability distribution
before sampling: values below 1.0 concentrate probability mass on the most likely
tokens, increasing output consistency; values above 1.0 flatten the distribution,
increasing diversity at the cost of coherence.  Renze and Guven (2024) conducted
a systematic empirical study of temperature effects on nine LLMs across five
prompt-engineering strategies and concluded that for analytical problem-solving
tasks, temperatures in the range 0.0–1.0 produce no statistically significant
difference in accuracy, justifying the use of any value in this range [32].
The present research uses temperature = 0.2 to favour consistent structured
output whilst avoiding the degenerate repetition associated with very low
temperatures.

Top-p (nucleus) sampling, introduced by Holtzman et al. (2020), restricts
sampling at each step to the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability
mass meets a threshold p [33].  The technique eliminates the unreliable low-
probability tail, the region from which semantically incoherent tokens are
most likely to be sampled, whilst preserving sufficient diversity from the
high-probability mass.  Holtzman et al. demonstrated that p = 0.95 produces the
most coherent and human-like text generation across multiple evaluation metrics,
a finding that has since become the most widely adopted default across production
inference APIs.  The present research uses top_p = 0.95.

Top-k filtering, introduced by Fan et al. (2018), truncates the next-token
distribution to the k highest-probability tokens before sampling [34].  Fan et
al. found that k = 40 optimally balances coherence and expressiveness for
structured text generation tasks.  This value has since been adopted as the
canonical default by Ollama and major open-source inference frameworks and is
used without modification in the present research.

Repetition penalties, first motivated by the work of Welleck et al. (2020)
on unlikelihood training, suppress degenerate token repetition whilst
preserving the legitimate recurrence of domain-specific terminology across
multiple issue blocks [35].  A mild penalty of 1.1 was applied; frequency and
presence penalties were set to zero to avoid suppressing WCAG SC identifiers
and ARIA attribute names that legitimately recur across multiple violations.
2.8  Cognitive Load and Developer Productivity
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.8.1  Cognitive Load Theory
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Cognitive load theory, introduced by Sweller (1988) in the context of problem-
solving instruction, proposes that human working memory is a limited capacity
resource and that the effectiveness of any information-processing task is
constrained by the total cognitive load, the demand on working memory, that
the task imposes [36].  Sweller distinguished three components of cognitive load:
intrinsic load, which arises from the inherent complexity of the material and
cannot be reduced without simplifying the subject matter itself; extraneous load,
which arises from the way information is presented and can be reduced through
better interface and interaction design; and germane load, which arises from the
effort invested in forming and automating schemas, the productive learning
component that instructional design should seek to maximise.

Paas, Renkl, and Sweller (2003) extended this framework in an influential review,
arguing that empirical measurement of cognitive load is essential for evaluating
the effectiveness of instructional and interface designs and surveying the
available subjective, physiological, and performance-based measurement methods
[37].  The review concluded that subjective self-report instruments, whilst
susceptible to retrospective bias, provide the most practical and ecologically
valid measurement approach for complex professional tasks in which physiological
signals are difficult to obtain.

Applied to the present research, the cognitive load framework provides the
theoretical justification for the second research hypothesis (H2): if an IDE-
integrated accessibility assistant reduces the extraneous load associated with
consulting external WCAG documentation and interpreting opaque rule-engine
output, the remaining cognitive resources are available for the productive
germane-load activity of designing and implementing accessible components.  A
reduction in extraneous cognitive load is predicted to appear as a decrease in
the NASA-TLX subscales most associated with extraneous demand: Mental Demand,
Effort, and Frustration.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.8.2  The NASA Task Load Index
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The NASA Task Load Index (NASA-TLX) was developed by Hart and Staveland (1988)
through a systematic programme of empirical research at the NASA Ames Research
Centre involving pilots and air-traffic controllers performing complex procedural
tasks [38].  The instrument assesses subjective workload on six subscales: Mental
Demand, Physical Demand, Temporal Demand, Performance, Effort, and Frustration
Level.  Each subscale is rated on a 21-interval bipolar scale with verbal anchors
at each extreme; in the raw (unweighted) scoring variant, the raw workload score
is computed as the arithmetic mean of the six subscale ratings.

Hart (2006) reviewed twenty years of NASA-TLX application experience across
aviation, military, automotive, and consumer technology domains and concluded
that the unweighted raw TLX score performs comparably to the original weighted
TLXD variant in discriminating between conditions and in predicting objective
performance measures, whilst requiring significantly less administration time
and eliminating the inter-subject variability introduced by the pairwise
comparison weighting procedure [39].  The present research uses the raw TLX
scoring variant on these grounds.

The NASA-TLX has been extensively validated as a workload assessment instrument
in human-computer interaction research.  Its subscales have demonstrated
construct validity across a wide range of computer-based task types, and its
sensitivity to task complexity and tool quality has been demonstrated in numerous
studies comparing tooled and untooled performance conditions [38][39].  Its
six-subscale structure enables directional interpretation of workload findings:
a reduction in Mental Demand and Effort subscales with no change in Temporal
Demand would suggest that the tool reduces intellectual labour without materially
accelerating task completion, whilst reductions across all six subscales would
suggest a more comprehensive workload benefit.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.8.3  Cognitive Load in Developer Workflows
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The application of cognitive load theory to software development workflows has
attracted substantial research interest as the complexity of developer toolchains
has grown.  Research in this domain has consistently identified context switching, defined
as repeated transitions between different cognitive tasks, tools, or information
sources, as a primary source of extraneous cognitive load in developer workflows.

Hellendoorn et al. (2021) provided empirical evidence that long gaps between
related development activities are associated with productivity loss, and
argued that this effect is partly attributable to the working-memory cost of
reconstructing task context after an interruption [20].  In the accessibility
auditing context, the dominant sources of context-switching extraneous load
are: switching from the editor to a browser-based checker; interpreting the
checker's output, which typically provides a SC reference but not a contextually
specific remediation instruction; switching to the WCAG documentation to
understand the specific requirement; switching back to the editor to apply the
fix; and, if the fix is non-trivial, switching to the ARIA Authoring Practices
Guide for implementation guidance.  This sequence involves a minimum of five
context switches per violation discovered during a post-authorship review cycle.

IDE-integrated tools that surface contextually specific, code-level feedback
within the editor reduce this context-switching overhead by collapsing multiple
information-retrieval steps into a single interaction.  Beyond detection, a tool
that provides inline educational scaffolding, explaining precisely which WCAG 2.2
success criterion is violated, why the violation exists, and how it should be
remediated, serves an additional function: it progressively builds developer
knowledge about accessibility standards through situated, just-in-time delivery
rather than relying on developers to consult separate documentation.  This
educational dimension is a design objective of the extension developed in the
present study, targeting web designers and developers regardless of their prior
accessibility expertise.

The theoretical prediction is that participants using the extension will exhibit
lower NASA-TLX Mental Demand, Effort, and Frustration scores than participants
performing the same audit task relying on separate browser-based checkers and
documentation.  This prediction constitutes the empirically testable form of H2.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.8.4  Evaluation Metrics for Binary Classification Systems
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The quantitative evaluation of accessibility detection systems requires metrics
that are well-calibrated for binary classification under class imbalance.
The F1 score, the harmonic mean of precision and recall, is the standard
primary metric in information extraction and classification tasks, but Chicco
and Jurman (2020) demonstrated through systematic analysis that F1 has a
critical limitation: it ignores the true negative count entirely, making it
insensitive to the one performance dimension that increases as a model avoids
false positives against a large negative class [40].

In the accessibility evaluation context, the negative class (code patterns that
do not violate WCAG) vastly outnumbers the positive class (genuine violations)
in any real-world file.  A model that generates excessive false positives can
achieve a moderately high F1 score if its recall is high, even though the
excessive FP rate makes the model unusable in practice, every false alarm
erodes developer trust.  Chicco and Jurman (2020) showed that the Matthews
Correlation Coefficient (MCC) accounts for all four confusion-matrix cells
proportionally and produces reliable informativeness rankings even under
extreme class imbalance, recommending it as the primary metric for binary
classification evaluation [40].  The present research uses both F1 (as the
primary recall-sensitive metric) and MCC (as the primary imbalance-robust
metric) as co-dependent evaluation criteria.
2.9  Research Synthesis: Identified Gaps and Study Contribution
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The preceding sections have reviewed five distinct but converging bodies of
literature.  This section synthesises the identified gaps across these bodies
and articulates the specific contribution the present research makes to the field.

Gap 1: The Semantic Detection Ceiling of Rule-Based Tools.  Section 2.4
established that automated DOM-based accessibility checkers (axe-core, WAVE,
Lighthouse, Pa11y) have a fundamental inability to evaluate the semantic adequacy
of ARIA attributes, accessible names, and dynamic state management.  The
theoretical and empirical evidence reviewed by Abou-Zahra et al. [17] and
Campoverde-Molina et al. [18] quantifies this ceiling at 25-40 per cent of
violations detectable by human experts.  The ground-truth validation conducted
in the present study (1.2% pattern-match accuracy for semantic violations)
confirms and sharpens this finding.  No prior study was identified that addresses
this ceiling through integration of a large language model into the IDE editing
workflow.

Gap 2: The Absence of WCAG 2.2-Aware IDE Tooling.  The WCAG 2.2 specification,
published in October 2023, introduced nine new success criteria addressing
previously uncovered interaction patterns (focus appearance, dragging movements,
target size, accessible authentication).  At the time of writing, no IDE plugin
or integrated developer tool was identified in this review that provides
real-time analysis specifically covering the WCAG 2.2 Level A and AA success
criteria for all four principal web development languages (HTML, CSS, JavaScript,
and TypeScript with JSX).  Existing IDE accessibility plugins confine their scope
to HTML and to the axe-core rule set, an architecture inherited from browser-
based tooling that cannot be straightforwardly extended to CSS logic or JavaScript
state-management analysis.

Gap 3: Missing RAG Application to Accessibility.  Section 2.6 demonstrated
that RAG substantially improves LLM factual accuracy on knowledge-intensive tasks
by grounding model outputs in authoritative external documentation [25].
No prior published work was identified that applies the RAG architecture to
accessibility analysis, either in an IDE-embedded tool or in a benchmarking
study.  The selection, construction, and evaluation of a domain-specific WCAG 2.2
knowledge base for hybrid retrieval therefore represents a novel contribution.

Gap 4: Absence of Multi-Model Comparative Benchmarking for Accessibility.
No prior study was identified that benchmarks a large cohort of diverse LLM
families on a standardised, ground-truth-validated accessibility detection task.
Studies on LLM code-analysis capabilities have focused on code generation
(Codex, HumanEval), bug detection, or general code review, none of which
exercises the WCAG-specific semantic reasoning required for accessibility auditing.
The present study's 18-model, 4-condition, 41-fixture evaluation provides the
first comparative benchmark of this scope for LLM-based WCAG 2.2 detection.

Gap 5: Absence of Cognitive-Load Evidence for Accessibility Tooling.  Section
2.8 reviewed the NASA-TLX instrument and the developer cognitive-load literature.
No prior study was identified that measures the cognitive workload cost of a
web developer accessibility audit task with and without AI assistance, nor that
correlates an AI tool's detection accuracy with developer workload reduction.  The
planned user study described in Chapter 3 (Section 3.4.4) is designed to address
this gap directly, providing the first empirical evidence linking accessibility
AI tool accuracy to NASA-TLX workload scores in a controlled developer task.

The present research addresses all five identified gaps through a single,
integrated study.  The artefact (the VS Code extension) addresses Gaps 1, 2,
and 3 simultaneously.  The evaluation framework (the 18-model ablation study)
addresses Gap 4.  The planned empirical user study (Section 3.4.4) addresses
Gap 5.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2.10  Chapter Conclusion
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This chapter has reviewed the bodies of literature that motivate, enable, and
contextualise the present research.  The review began with the empirical evidence
for the persistence of web accessibility barriers at scale, a global problem
affecting 1.3 billion people that existing automated tooling has demonstrably
failed to solve, and that legislation in the EU, UK, and US is increasingly
mandating organisations to address.  It then surveyed WCAG 2.2 and WAI-ARIA as
the technical standards that define the problem space; examined the current
generation of automated evaluation tools and characterised their detection
ceiling; and established the semantic accessibility gap that motivates the turn
to language model-based analysis.

The review then traced the development of large language models from the
transformer architecture through GPT-3's few-shot capabilities and Codex's
code intelligence to the emerging application of LLMs to accessibility auditing.
The RAG architecture was reviewed as the mechanism for addressing the hallucination
and knowledge-currency limitations of purely parametric models.  Hybrid BM25
and dense-semantic retrieval was identified as the technically appropriate
retrieval design for the structured WCAG knowledge base.

Prompt engineering research, particularly chain-of-thought prompting and its
zero-shot variant, was reviewed as the technique for eliciting structured,
confidence-gated analytical outputs from the model.  The inference sampling
parameters governing response quality were reviewed with reference to their
empirical foundations, providing the theoretical basis for the parameter choices
made in Chapter 3.

Finally, this chapter reviewed cognitive load theory, the NASA-TLX instrument,
and the developer productivity literature to establish the theoretical and
measurement framework for the human-factors evaluation strand of the study.
The Matthews Correlation Coefficient was reviewed as the preferred primary metric
for binary classification under class imbalance, providing the rationale for its
co-primary role alongside F1 in the benchmarking evaluation.

The synthesis in Section 2.9 identified five distinct gaps across the reviewed
literature, all of which the present research is designed to address, and
articulated the novel contribution of the study at each gap.  Chapter 3 proceeds
to describe the methodology and implementation through which these contributions
are realised.
